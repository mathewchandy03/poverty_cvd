\begin{abstract}
Heart disease is the leading cause of death in the United States and globally. It falls under the broader umbrella of diseases referred to as cardiovascular diseases, which constitutes 1 in 3 deaths in the United States. Therefore, it is of great interest to understand potential causes of cardiovascular disease mortality. These can take the form of more individual variables like level of nutrition and exercise as well as societal factors. The social force of poverty is known to have a link with many negative outcomes, one of these being cardiovascular disease mortality. However, the exact relationship is confounded by many other covariates. We use recent methods developed for continuous treatments to estimate the total causal effect that poverty rate has on the cardiovascular disease mortality rate of a county in the U.S.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

Understanding the causal relationship between poverty
and CVD mortality and the heterogeneity across different levels of covariates could provide insights that would better inform our understanding of how underlying societal structures 
intersect with health outcomes. In this paper, our findings suggest how certain covariates may make poverty's impact on CVD mortality worse, outline how currently existing policies may be weakening the effect of poverty, and enable us to make recommendations on future policy.
In this study, we specifically investigate the causal
relationship between poverty rate ($X$) and
CVD mortality rate per 1000 people ($Y$)at the county level. 

We also consider additional covariates including percentage of people aged 65 and above ($A$), percentage of people who do not identify as Non-Hispanic White ($C$),
percentage of people who are uninsured ($M$), Medicaid expansion of the state which the county is a part of ($G$), and
urban-rural classification ($R$) according to the 2013 National Center for Health Statistics \citep{ingram20142013}.

Despite extensive literature linking poverty and health outcomes, there is limited work estimating continuous-treatment causal effects at the county level while accounting for both confounding and missing outcome mechanisms. This paper addresses this gap.
For causal effect estimation, many strategies were considered.
The simplest would be to binarize poverty rate into two levels. However, doing this would lose the high variability in poverty rate there is among U.S. counties. For example, grouping moderately poor counties with severely impoverished ones could obscure meaningful variation in treatment effects.
One alternative is the partially linear model \citep{chernozhukov2018double} within the double machine learning (DML) framework, which is a highly interpretable method that treats the relationship between a treatment and an outcome as linear while quantifying the covariate-outcome relationship with a more complicated machine learning model. While simple to implement and understand, it suffers
from its rigid linear assumption, when a continuous treatment may have a richer causal relationship.
\citet{kennedy2017non} offered a flexible non-parametric
way to get around this, while still affording high interpretability. A similar method, however, has not yet
been developed to account for mediation or to estimate
effect on sub-populations. For this latter question,
we utilize the popular causal forest \citep{wager2018estimation, athey2019generalized}, and answer interesting questions concerning how the total effect changes for different covariate values.
Our contributions are:
(1) application of doubly robust continuous-treatment causal effect estimators to county-level health data,
(2) A censored-imputation strategy aligned with known suppression mechanisms and sensitivity analysis on semi-synthetic data,
(3) A heterogeneity analysis via causal forests.

In Section~\ref{causal_model}, we outline the assumptions of our causal model on which we based our analysis. In 
Section~\ref{data}, we discuss some challenges encountered with this dataset and how we handled missing data. In Section~\ref{sensitivity}, we conduct a sensitivity analysis to highlight the superior performance of the multiple imputation method we chose. In Section~\ref{quantities}, we discuss the identification problem of the causal quantity of interest.
Section~\ref{estimation}, we describe our DML procedure
to identify the total effect of poverty rate on cardiovascular mortality rate. In
Section~\ref{heterogeneity}, we utilize a causal 
forest to conduct an analysis of causal effect heterogeneity. Finally, we discuss our conclusions
in Section~\ref{discussion}.


\section{Causal Model}
\label{causal_model}

Let us write out our causal model, where each county is an observational unit.
Let $\mathbf V = \{X, Y, A, C, M, G, R, P, R_Y\}$, and
let $\mathbf  U = \{U_X, U_Y, U_A, U_C,U_M,U_G,U_R, U_P\}$,
where $X \leftarrow U_X$, $Y \leftarrow U_Y$,
$A \leftarrow U_A$, $C \leftarrow U_C$, $M \leftarrow U_M$,  
$G \leftarrow U_G$, and $R \leftarrow U_R$.
Here is a diagram of our assumed causal relationship
model, omitting $P$ and $R_Y$ for now:

\noindent

Here is a short review of literature concerning
the causal relationships claimed in our causal diagram.
Poverty directly increases CVD mortality through stress,
malnutrition, and poor housing pathways independent of uninsurance \citep{kim2025mediating}. Poverty also reduces
healthcare access by pricing workers out of employer-sponsored and individual-market coverage, confirmed by the Affordable Care Act (ACA) Medicaid expansion difference-in-differences literature \citep{khatana2019association}, which also supports medicaid expansion as a cause of reduction in both uninsurance and CVD mortality directly and higher uninsurance rates as a cause of increase in CVD mortality. These conclusions are also supported by \citet{miller2021medicaid}, which links Medicaid expansion
to a 9.4\% reduction in annual mortality using individual-level linked survey and death records. 

Racial composition increases county poverty through structural racism operating via historically accumulated barriers in housing, employment, and lending \citep{bailey2021structural}. Racial composition also independently raises uninsurance rates - disparities persist within income an deducation groups, confirming this edge is not fully mediated by poverty \citep{lee2021convergence}. Lastly, racial composition increases CVD mortality independently of the poverty and uninsurance pathways, with \citet{geronimus2006weathering}
documenting widening racial gaps in biological stress accumulation that are not explained by income. Rural counties typically have higher poverty due to fewer labor markets and potential industrial decline \citep{farrigan2026rural} and higher CVD mortality through
hospital closures and scarcity of cardiologists that may be independent of poverty \citep{loccoh2022rural}.

The level of urbanicity also raises uninsurance rates through lower employer-sponsored coverage availability
and provider network shortages, though the raw uninsurance gap is smaller than the access gap, so this edge is partially but imperfectly captured by the SAHIE uninsurance measure. Finally, how old the population of a county is predicts CVD mortality as a precision variable. We also include a bidirected edge between urbanicity and non-white-population, with racial minorities making up about 22 percent of the American rural population in 2018, and 43 percent of the urban population \citep{castillo2020racial},
and one between urbanicity and county age, supported a trend of ageing in rural counties \citep{monnat2025us, cohen2023aging}.

We also offer a discussion of how we handled missing response data in our causal model. Because of public data documentation \citet{cdc_wonder_cmf_doc},
we do not have to guess the $m$-graph because
the mechanism is deterministic. It is explicitly
stated that mortality rates are suppressed ($R_Y$) if the total
number of deaths $D$ is less than 10:
$$R_Y \leftarrow \mathds{1} \{D < 10\}$$
The number of deaths
$D$ is a product of the population and the crude
mortality rate, which we know can be expressed as a function of age
and adjusted mortality rate. To show these relationships,
the modified DAG is presented below.

\section{Data}
\label{data}

Because it is the latest year with publicly available CVD mortality data, we focus on 2016 data from several sources which we merge
by county FIPS code.
The first is the CDC Wonder dataset \citep{cdc_wonder_cmf_data}, which contains
information on age-adjusted CVD mortality rate ($Y$) for counties across the U.S. We use the age-adjusted outcome,
which standardizes to the year-2000 national population, to remove the mechanical effect of county age structure on mortality rates \citep{anderson1998age}.
The second is the American Community Survey, which contains
information on poverty rate ($X$), non-white population ($C$), which serves as a proxy for general racial composition, and \% aged 65 years older ($A$) 
which we retained as a covariate despite the age adjustment because standardization to the year-2000 reference population does not fully eliminate residual age confounding in counties with extreme demographic profiles.
We use the Small Area Health Insurance Estimates (SAHIE)
to obtain uninsurance rate ($M$) and the National Center for Health Statistics (NCHS) to obtain the urbanicity classification ($R$) as defined in 2013.  The urban-rural classification scheme has six ordinal levels:  
large central metro, large fringe metro, medium metro,
small metro, micropolitan, and noncore, where noncore is the most rural and large central metro is the most urban.
Each of these levels is determined by the population and whether the county is part of a larger metropolitan area, smaller micropolitan area, or neither. The way that counties are grouped into these areas is a more involved process which accounts for commuting patterns \citep{office20212020}. This scheme was chosen over population density because it captures functional economic areas and healthcare access patterns more directly than raw density measures.

% https://www.cdc.gov/nchs/data/data-analysis/2023-File-Documentation-final.pdf. 
%https://www.census.gov/content/dam/Census/library/visualizations/2021/demo/p30-09/F2_Map.pdf
We obtain each county's medicaid expansion status ($G$) as of January 1st, 2016 from KFF.

The observational units in this study were U.S. counties and county-equivalents, not including territories, former independent cities, 
and planned regions. Census areas in the unincorporated
borough of Alaska required some special consideration because of potential renaming, merges, and splits that may have occurred. After accounting for these county-level challenges, we were left with 3,142 counties and
county-equivalents.

Five rows were removed because they were labelled
as having "missing" CVD mortality rates, as opposed to the
168 rows that were labelled as "suppressed", indicating
that the number of deaths was lower than 10.
As shown in Section~\ref{causal_model}, CVD mortality
is classified as missing not at random (MNAR) because $\mathbf V_m \not\!\perp\!\!\!\perp R_Y|\mathbf V_o$,
where $\mathbf V_m=Y$ (which is partially missing) and
$\mathbf V_o = \{X,A,C,M,G,R,P\}$. In other words, the joint distribution is not recoverable because $Y \in \text {pa}(R_Y)$.
Consequently, because $\mathbb P(\mathbf V)$ cannot
be identified, the causal effect $\mathbb E[Y_x]$ is
not $m$-identifiable, and we cannot use non-parametric
causal recovery methods.

Although the missingness mechanism depends on the unobserved outcome, it is not arbitrary. The suppression rule is deterministic and reveals that the true outcome lies within a known interval. Therefore, the problem can be reframed as one of interval censoring rather than generic missing not at random (MNAR).
However, because the exact value of the outcome within this interval is not observed, the joint distribution of the data is not still nonparametrically identified. Our approach therefore relies on a parametric model for the conditional distribution of the outcome within the censoring bounds. Therefore, we proceed under a parametric modeling assumption via left-censored imputation. Thus, our results should be interpreted as relying on the correctness of the imputation model rather than purely identification from observed data.

We utilize the multiple imputation by chained equations
(MICE) algorithm with an interval-censored (left-bounded and right-truncated) outcome model, where each draw is bounded between 0 and the county-specific theoretical maximum limit of detection $\text{LOD}_i =9/P_i$, ensuring that all imputed values match the known deterministic missingness mechanism. This procedure is based on \citet{keil2019quantile}
and uses the corresponding \texttt{qgcomp} package.
Although the suppression threshold is triggered by raw death counts, we impute the age-adjusted mortality rate
by including county-level age demographic variables in
the MICE predictor matrix, allowing the conditionally
draw distributions to somewhat account for each county's true age structure. The pseudo-code of the multiple imputation procedure is presented in Algorithm~\ref{alg:mice}.





\begin{algorithm}[t]
   \caption{Multiple Imputation of Left-Censored CVD Mortality Rates}
   \label{alg:mice}
\begin{algorithmic}
   \STATE {\bfseries Input:} $(x_i,y_i,\mathbf z_i, P_i), i=1,...,n$, number of imputations $M=5$, maximum iterations $I=10$
   \FOR{$i=1$ {\bfseries to} $n$}
        \STATE $y_i \leftarrow \max(0, 0.1 / P_i)$ \hfill
       \IF{$y_i$ is suppressed}
           \STATE $\text{LOD}_i \leftarrow 9 / P_i$ \hfill
       \ENDIF
   \ENDFOR
   \STATE Let $f(y_i \mid x_i, \mathbf l_i)$ denote the conditional log-normal density.
   \FOR{$m=1$ {\bfseries to} $M$}
       \STATE Draw missing $\{y_i^{(m)}\} \sim f(y_i \mid x_i, \mathbf l_i)$ right-truncated at $LOD_i$ via chained equations for $I$ iterations.
   \ENDFOR
   \STATE {\bfseries Output:}  $\{(x_i^{(m)},y_i^{(m)},\mathbf z_i^{(m)}, P_i)\}_{i=1}^n$, $m = 1,...,M$
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

In the first pass, we apply a correction to observed zero mortality rates to stabilize the log-likelihood.
We specify the fully conditional imputation model, $f(y_i|x_i, \mathbf l_i)$ as a log-normal distribution which captures the strictly positive and right-skewed nature of mortality rates. For each of $m \in \{1,...,M\}$ imputed datasets, the missing values are drawn via Gibbs sampling for $I = 10$ iterations from 
their conditional log-normal distribution, right-truncated at their respective $\text{LOD}_i$ limits.
The procedure output $M$ datasets to be passed into
our estimation and heterogeneity techniques.

\section{Sensitivity Analysis}
\label{sensitivity}

To illustrate that the left-censored MICE method may
offer improved performance relative to complete case analysis (CCA) and
standard MICE, we conduct a simulation study based on semi-synthetic data.
Specifically, we generate an age-adjusted CVD mortality outcome $Y$, the corresponding unadjusted mortality $Y_{\text{raw}}$, and an indicator $R_Y$ denoting whether the outcome is suppressed.

According to the CDC WONDER documentation on age-adjusted mortality rates \citep{cdc_wonder_cmf_doc}, denoting $\mathcal{K}$ as the set of age groups, the age-adjusted CVD mortality is calculated as:
$$
\sum_{k\in\mathcal{K}} (Ps_k/Ps) Y_{\text{raw}, k},
$$
where $Ps_k$ is the standard population for age group $k$, $Ps$ is the total standard population, and $Y_{\text{raw}, k}$ is the raw CVD mortality for age group $k$. In our synthetic data, we consider two age groups: individuals younger than 65 years old ($k=1$) and those aged 65 or older ($k=2$). For simplicity, we use the empirical population distribution from the 2016 data as the reference population to construct the weights $(Ps_k/Ps)$. We generate raw CVD mortality for individuals younger than 65 in each county as
$Y_{\text{raw},1} \sim \mathcal{N}(\mu_1, 2^2)$,
with $\mu_1 = 1 + 15X + 5C + 4M - 0.2R - 0.2G$
and for individuals aged 65 or older as
$Y_{\text{raw},2} \sim \mathcal{N}(\mu_2, 4^2)$, with $\mu_2 = 10 + 15X + 5C + 4M - 0.2R - 0.2G.$
To ensure non-negativity of mortality rates, we truncate both $Y_{\text{raw},1}$ and $Y_{\text{raw},2}$ at zero by replacing negative values with zero. 
The overall (crude) mortality rate $Y_{\text{raw}}$ is constructed as a population-weighted average of the two age-specific mortality rates. The age-adjusted outcome $Y$ is then computed using fixed weights corresponding to the overall population proportions in each age group.
To mimic suppression in publicly available data, we define an indicator $R_Y = \mathbb{I}(D < 10)$, where $D = P \cdot Y_{\text{raw}} / 1000$ is the expected number of deaths in a county. Outcomes with $D < 10$ are treated as suppressed, and the corresponding values of $Y$ are set to missing.
For each of 100 replications, we generate a semi-synthetic dataset and apply three estimation strategies: (i) standard MICE with five imputations, (ii) left-censored MICE using a Tobit imputation model with a lower detection limit corresponding to 9 deaths, and (iii) complete case analysis (CCA). For each method, we estimate the regression coefficient of $X$ in a linear model of $Y$ on $X$, $C$, $M$, $R$, and $G$, and compute the bias relative to the true coefficient $\beta = 15$.
From Figure~\ref{fig:sensitivity}, we observe that while left-censored MICE exhibits a small amount of residual bias, it substantially reduces bias compared to both standard MICE and CCA. In particular, standard MICE fails to account for the censoring mechanism induced by suppression, and CCA suffers from selection bias due to the systematic exclusion of low-count counties. These results support the use of left-censored MICE for handling suppressed outcomes in our empirical analysis.

\section{Causal Quantity of Interest}
\label{quantities}

In this section, we outline the procedure we conducted to analyze the causal effect of poverty rate on CVD mortality.
Many variables in this study, including the treatment and the response, are continuous. This necessitates slightly
different notation and analysis than in the binary case. Instead of considering only two levels $x_0$ and $x_1$ of the treatment poverty rate, we want to consider the effect at any arbitrary level $x_0$ which would enable us to identify
$x_1 = x_0 + \delta$, where $\delta$ represents any change in the state of $X$.
For instance,
the total variation can be stated as
$$TV_{x_1,x_0}(y)=\mathbb E[Y|X=x_1]-\mathbb E[Y|X=x_0]$$

In variation analysis, the primary objective is to isolate the direct effect,
indirect effect, and spurious effect. Because accounting for mediation is non-trivial in this setting and remains a larger problem unaddressed in the literature, we do not estimate it in this paper, but leave the question as room for future work. We restate the queries of potential interest in formal notation and in the language of the domain.
Below, $Y_x = Y|\text{do}(X=x)$, or $Y$ when the treatment $X$ is set to
level $x$. Thus, $\mathbb E[Y_x]$ represents the expected CVD mortality if all counties were set to poverty rate $x$, averaging over the observed covariate distribution.


\begin{enumerate}
    \item How much does age-adjusted CVD mortality change,
allowing all pathways (healthcare access + stress + structural factors) to operate?
    $$TE_{x_1,x_0}(y) = \mathbb E[Y_{x_1}]-\mathbb E[Y_{x_0}]$$
%     \item How much does age-adjusted CVD mortality change,
% isolating pathways (stress, housing, nutrition, structural racism channels, etc.) that do not operate through reduced healthcare?
% $$NDE_{x_0,x_1}(y) = \mathbb E[Y_{x_1, M_{x_0}}]-\mathbb E[Y_{x_0}]$$
%     \item How much does age-adjusted CVD mortality change,
% isolating the indirect pathway through uninsurance?
% $$NIE_{x_1,x_0}(y) = \mathbb E[Y_{x_1, M_{x_0}}]-\mathbb E[Y_{x_1}]$$
    \item How much of the poverty–mortality association is driven by
racial composition,
rurality,
Medicaid expansion, and 
age structure
rather than poverty itself?
    $$SE_{x_1,x_0}=\mathbb E[Y_{x_1}|X=x_0] - \mathbb E[Y_{x_1}|X=x_1]$$
\end{enumerate}

We focus on total variation, total effect,
and spurious effect (which is just the difference) in this paper. In the continuous setting, we use notation without subscript to refer to the effect as a function at level $x$. Formally, $TV(x) = \mathbb E[Y|X=x]$, $TE(x) = \mathbb E[Y_x]$, and $SE(x) = \mathbb E[Y|X=x] - \mathbb E[Y_x]$, the last of which corresponds to the confounding-induced gap between observational and interventional means. Note that $SE(x) = TV(x) - TE(x)$; the direction was switched to ease interpretation. 





Because of the causal relationship $TE(x) = TV(x) - SE(x)$, 
meaning that the minimal procedure requires us to only estimate $TV(x)$ and $TE(x)$, and $SE(x)$ can
be inferred from the vertical distance between
$TV(x)$ and $TE(x)$.

We require the following standard assumptions for identification (\citep{kennedy2017non}) of $TE(x)$. Let $\mathcal X$ and $\mathcal Z$ be the supports of $X$ and $Z$, respectively. For some treatment space $\mathcal X$, they must hold for any $x \in \mathcal X$.
\begin{assumption}[Consistency]
$X=x \rightarrow Y = Y_x$
\end{assumption}

\begin{assumption}[Positivity]
$\pi(x|\mathbf z) \geq \pi_{min} > 0,\forall \ \mathbf z \in \mathcal Z$
\end{assumption}

\begin{assumption}[Ignorability]
$\mathbb E[Y_x|\mathbf Z,X] = \mathbb E[Y_x|\mathbf Z]$
\end{assumption}
We can use the backdoor adjustment set $\mathbf Z= \{C,R\}$, which blocks the back-door paths $X\leftarrow C \rightarrow Y$, $X\leftarrow C \rightarrow M\rightarrow Y$, $X \leftarrow R \rightarrow Y$, and 
$X \leftarrow R \rightarrow M\rightarrow Y$. 
While $\{C,R\}$ constitutes the minimal adjustment set
for identifying $TE(x)$, I utilize the expanded set $\mathbf Z = \{C,R,A,G\}$, including $A$ and $G$ as precision variables. Importantly, $M$ is not included in the adjustment set because it is a mediator, but we include it in the causal model for completeness. Then
the causal effect of $X$ can

Then the causal effect of $X$ can be expressed via backdoor adjustment as:
$$\mathbb{E}[Y_x] = \int_\mathcal Z \mathbb{E}[Y \mid X = x, \mathbf Z = \mathbf z] dP(\mathbf z),$$
where $P(\mathbf z)$ is the distribution of $\mathbf Z$.

\section{Estimation of Causal Effect}
\label{estimation}

An estimate of the total variation $\widehat {TV}(x)$ can be obtained
with naive nonparametric regression on $X$ alone without adjusting for any other covariates. In our application, we used local linear regression with a Gaussian kernel where the bandwidth $h$ was selected by minimizing the cross-validated leave-one-out cross-validation score:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:cv}
    CV(h)= \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n\Big (\frac{Y_i-\hat Y_i(h)}{1-h_{ii}}\Big)^2,
\end{equation}
where $h_{ii}$ is the $i$th diagonal element of the hat matrix.
% Assuming a linear relationship, $TV$ would be represented by $\beta_{TV}$ below:
% $$Y=\beta_0 + \beta_{TV} X + \epsilon,$$
% and could be estimated with $\hat \beta_{TV}$ from ordinary least squares. 
An estimate of the total effect $\widehat {TE}$ is slightly more complicated in the continuous setting, and necessitates newer causal tools. We make use of
the framework developed by \citet{kennedy2017non}. First,
let's denote the mean outcome given observed covariates $\mathbf z$ and treatment $x$ with $\mu(\mathbf z, x) = \mathbb E[Y|\mathbf Z =\mathbf z, X=x]$, which allows us to rewrite the causal effect:
$$\mathbb E[Y_x]= \mathbb E[\mu(\mathbf Z, x)] = \int_\mathcal Z \mu(\mathbf z, x)dP(\mathbf z)$$ 
Further, let's denote the marginal treatment density as $\bar \omega(x) = \partial \mathbb P(X \leq x)/\partial x$ and 
the conditional treatment density given covariates 
with $\pi(x|\mathbf z)= \partial \mathbb P(X \leq x|\mathbf Z = \mathbf z) / \partial x$.


In this setting, we want to obtain a function $\xi(\mathbf V;\pi,\mu)$ of observed data $\mathbf V$ and nuisiance functions
$(\pi, \mu)$
such that
$$\mathbb E[\xi(\mathbf V;\bar\pi,\bar\mu)|X=x]=\mathbb E[Y_x],$$
if at least one of $\bar \pi = \pi$ and $\bar \mu  = \mu$.
Then it follows that $\mathbb E[\xi(\mathbf V;\bar\pi,\bar\mu)] = \psi$, 
where
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:influence}
    \psi = \int_\mathcal X\int_\mathcal Z\mu(\mathbf z, x) \bar \omega(x)dP(\mathbf z)dx.
\end{equation}
After having estimated $\hat \mu$ and $\hat \pi$ with machine learning methods, we can regress $\xi(\mathbf V;\hat\pi,\hat\mu)$ on
$X$ alone as we did to estimate total variation.
We present the following result from \citet{kennedy2017non} regarding
the efficient influence function, which we want to be doubly robust in
that the expectation is zero.

\begin{theorem}[Efficient Influence Function \citep{kennedy2017non}]
    Under a non-parametric model, the efficient influence function for $\psi$ as defined in Equation~\ref{eq:influence} is $\xi(\mathbf V;\pi,\mu) - \psi + \int_\mathcal X\{ \mu(\mathbf Z, x) - \int_\mathcal Z \mu(\mathbf z, x)d P(\mathbf z)\}\bar \omega (x)dx$, with
    \begin{multline*}
        \xi(\mathbf V; \pi, \mu)=\frac{Y-\mu(\mathbf Z, X)}{\pi (X|\mathbf Z)}\int_\mathcal Z \pi(X|\mathbf z)d P(\mathbf z)+\\
    \int_\mathcal Z \mu(\mathbf z, X)d P(\mathbf z)
    \end{multline*}
\end{theorem}
    
This allows us to estimate the effect curve by estimating the nuisance functions and then the following pseudo-outcome, which we regress on the treatment:
\begin{multline}
\label{eq:pseudo}
    \hat \xi(\mathbf V; \hat \pi, \hat \mu)=\frac{Y-\hat \mu(\mathbf Z, X)}{\hat \pi (X|\mathbf Z)}\int_\mathcal Z \hat\pi(X|\mathbf z)d \mathbb P_n(\mathbf z)+\\
    \int_\mathcal Z \hat \mu(\mathbf z, X)d \mathbb P_n(\mathbf z)
\end{multline}
Here $\mathbb P_n$ is the empirical measure.

To appropriately quantify the uncertainty in our non-parametric effect estimates, 
we construct sandwich-style pointwise confidence bands based directly on the empirical covariance of the efficient influence function components. For a given evaluation point $x$, let $\hat{\Sigma}^{(m)}(x)$ denote the pointwise variance of the causal effect estimated from this sandwich approach for the $m$-th imputed dataset. 

To appropriately quantify the uncertainty in our non-parametric estimates,
we construct sandwich-style pointwise confidence bands based directly on the
empirical covariance of the efficient influence function components for the
total effect, and use standard local linear asymptotic variance for the
total variation. Let $\hat{\Sigma}_{TE}^{(m)}(x)$ and
$\hat{\Sigma}_{TV}^{(m)}(x)$ denote these pointwise variances at evaluation
point $x$ for the $m$-th imputed dataset.

We aggregate the pointwise estimates across $M$ imputations using
Rubin's Rules \citep{rubin1987multiple}. Let
$\widehat{TE}(x) = \frac{1}{M}\sum_{m=1}^M \widehat{TE}^{(m)}(x)$
be the pooled point estimate. The total pointwise variance is given by
$V_{TE}(x) = W_{TE}(x) + \left(1 + \frac{1}{M}\right)B_{TE}(x)$,
where $W_{TE}$ is the average within-imputation variance and $B_{TE}$ is
the between-imputation variance. The same pooling procedure is applied to
the total variation components to obtain $V_{TV}(x)$. The final $95\%$
confidence intervals at each point $x$ are constructed as:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ci}
    \widehat{TE}(x) \pm 1.96 \sqrt{V_{TE}(x)} \quad \text{and} \quad
    \widehat{TV}(x) \pm 1.96 \sqrt{V_{TV}(x)}
\end{equation}

Notably, because the total variation is a naive regression of $Y$ on $X$,
its residual error term absorbs all the variance driven by the complex
covariates in $\mathbf{Z}$. Conversely, the pseudo-outcome used for the
total effect has already adjusted the variance explained by the
nuisance models $\hat{\mu}$ and $\hat{\pi}$. As a result, at extreme
values of $X$ where data is sparse, the unadjusted noise in the naive
estimator causes its standard errors to expand dramatically, whereas the
predictive stability of the doubly robust pseudo-outcome keeps the total
effect confidence bands much tighter.

In practice, this requires the training of three models. The conditional expectation $\hat \mu(\mathbf Z, X)$ is easy enough to estimate with
any machine learning model. The conditional density $\pi(x|\mathbf z)$ is not as
straightforward in comparison. Whereas in the binary treatment case, where we could predict a single probability from 0 to 1, we need a fully specified
conditional probability density function. This is
non-trivial, so it is broken down into a "location-scale" model with two separate regressions.
We first regress the treatment $X$ on the covariates $\mathbf Z$. The predictions from this model give
us the expected treatment level for a specific county,
and we then compute the residuals from model predictions:
$$\hat \mu_X(\mathbf Z) = \mathbb E[X|\mathbf Z]$$
$$\hat \epsilon=X-\hat \mu_X(\mathbf Z).$$
Secondly, we regress the squared residuals $\hat \epsilon^2_i$ on the covariates $\mathbf Z_i$ for $i \in \{1,...,n\}$, which
returns the conditional variance of the treatment:
$$\hat \sigma^2_X(\mathbf Z) = \mathbb E[\hat \epsilon^2|\mathbf Z].$$



For each of these three models, we applied a separate XGBoost model to our data
using the \texttt{SuperLearner} package \citep{SuperLearner}, which performs 10-fold cross-validation internally. In our analysis, the cross-fitted nuisance models are precision-weighted by county population to account for heteroskedasticity. As stated in Section~\ref{data}, mortality rate
derived from low underlying death counts are considered
statistically unreliable due to high 
relative standard errors. This volatility is likely made
worse by the age-adjustment process itself. Because
age-adjusted rates are computed by applying county-level age-specific death rates to a standard population structure, small counties often have age groups with
low populations, making the resulting standardized rates
unstable. Weighting the models by total population helps us to overcome this issue and avoid high-variance from
small counties.

To construct the doubly robust pseudo-outcome
from these pieces, we generate a matrix of counterfactual predictions. In other words, we predict the outcome for
all $n$ counties across a fixed grid of potential poverty levels across the observed range. Let us refer to this grid as $\mathcal X_{\text{eval}}$. This effectively forces every county to experience each level of poverty while
holding their baseline covariates constant. We then
take the column means of the matrix, which is equivalent to integrating out the covariates by averaging over the
empirical distribution.
We then fit a cubic smoothing spline to interpolate
these marginalized values at each county's actually observed
poverty level $X_i$:
$$\int_\mathbf z\hat \mu(\mathbf z,X_i)d \mathbb P_n(\mathbf z)=\frac{1}{n}\sum_{j=1}^n \hat\mu(\mathbf Z_j,X_i)$$
To estimate the conditional treatment density non-parametrically without relying on a strict Gaussian assumption, we apply kernel density estimation (KDE) to the residuals of the location-scale model. We compute
the standardized treatment values by centering the poverty rates around their conditionally predicted means and scaling them by their conditionally predicting standard deviations:
$$\tilde \epsilon_i=\frac{\hat \epsilon_i}{\hat\sigma_X(\mathbf Z_i)}=\frac{X_i-\hat \mu_X(\mathbf Z_i)}{\hat \sigma_X(\mathbf Z_i)}.$$
We estimate the empirical probability density function of these standardized residuals, and fit a cubic smoothing
spline again to estimate the KDE curve at the specific
standardized values of our observations. This extracts $\hat \pi(X_i|\mathbf Z_i)$, which represents the estimated conditional density of the treatment evaluated at each county's observed poverty level. Similar to 
the construction of the marginal outcome, we also compute the marginalized treatment density. We generate a matrix of counterfactual density predictions by standardizing
and evaluating the KDE across $\{\tilde \epsilon_i\}_{i=1}^n$
for all $n$ counties. We once again integrate out the 
baseline covariates by taking the column means, and
fit a final spline to interpolate these marginal
expectations to the observed poverty levels:
$$\int_\mathbf z\hat \pi (X_i | \mathbf z)d \mathbb P_n(\mathbf z)=\frac{1}{n}\sum_{j=1}^n \hat\pi(X_i|\mathbf Z_j)$$
We have now shown the how to obtain all the necessary pieces for the pseudo-outcome in practice.


In combination with our MICE setup, we now describe our full procedure for estimation of causal effects:


\begin{algorithm}[t]
   \caption{Total Effect Estimation with Doubly Robust Smoothing and Rubin's Rules}
   \label{alg:te}
\begin{algorithmic}
   \STATE {\bfseries Input:} Observed data $\{(X_i^{(m)},Y_i^{(m)},\mathbf Z_i^{(m)}, P_i)\}_{i=1}^n$ for imputations $m = 1,...,M$, evaluation grid $\mathcal{X}_{eval}$ 
   \FOR{$m=1$ {\bfseries to} $M$}
       \STATE Fit $\hat{\mu}_X(\mathbf{Z}) = \mathbb{E}[X|\mathbf{Z}]$, $\hat{\sigma}^2_X(\mathbf{Z}) = \max(\mathbb{E}[\hat\epsilon^2|\mathbf{Z}], 10^{-4})$, and $\hat{\mu}(\mathbf{Z}, X) = \mathbb{E}[Y|\mathbf{Z}, X]$ using population-weighted SuperLearner
       \STATE Standardize observed residuals: $\tilde{\epsilon}_i = \hat \epsilon_i / \hat{\sigma}_X(\mathbf{Z}_i)$ for $i=1,...,n$
       \STATE Estimate conditional density $\hat \pi(X_i|\mathbf Z_i)$ using KDE on $\{\tilde{\epsilon}_i\}_{i=1}^n$
       \FOR{$i=1$ {\bfseries to} $n$}
           \STATE Construct pseudo-outcome $\hat{\xi}^{(m)}_i$ as in Equation~\ref{eq:pseudo}
       \ENDFOR
       \STATE Find optimal bandwidths $h_{TV}^*$ and $h_{TE}^*$ via leave-one-out cross-validation
       \FOR{each grid point $x \in \mathcal{X}_{eval}$}
            \STATE Estimate $\widehat{TV}^{(m)}(x)$ via local linear
            regression of $Y$ on $X$ at $x$ 
            \STATE Estimate $\widehat{TE}^{(m)}(x)$ via local polynomial
            regression of $\hat{\xi}^{(m)}$ on $X$ at $x$
            \STATE Compute pointwise variances $\hat{\Sigma}_{TV}^{(m)}(x)$
            and $\hat{\Sigma}_{TE}^{(m)}(x)$
        \ENDFOR
    \ENDFOR
    \FOR{each grid point $x \in \mathcal{X}_{eval}$}
        \STATE Compute pooled estimates $\widehat{TE}(x)$ and $\widehat{TV}(x)$
        \STATE Compute pooled variances $V_{TE}(x)$ and $V_{TV}(x)$
        and 95\% CIs as in Equation~\ref{eq:ci}
    \ENDFOR
    \STATE {\bfseries Output:} Aggregated curves $\widehat{TV}(x)$
    and $\widehat{TE}(x)$ with 95\% confidence bands
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

The results from running this algorithm with $M = 5$ are shown in
Figure~\ref{fig:variation_analysis}. A naive analysis of total variation
would lead to the conclusion that poverty rate is associated with an
increase in age-adjusted CVD mortality up to a poverty rate of slightly
less than 40\%, at which point the effect appears to reverse. However,
our causal analysis reveals that CVD mortality actually plateaus near the
40\% poverty mark. This suggests diminishing marginal effects rather than
a reversal of the causal relationship. Importantly, the doubly robust
curve demonstrates that the isolated county-level effect of poverty on
CVD mortality is higher than the naive estimate initially suggests,
highlighting that the other covariates jointly confound the relationship
and artificially suppress the perceived effect at certain poverty levels.

\section{Causal Effect Heterogeneity}
\label{heterogeneity}
To offer an alternative approach to this problem and
to enable analysis of heterogeneity in causal effect,
we applied a causal forest using the \texttt{grf} package \citep{grf}. 
When the treatment is continuous, this estimates the conditional average treatment effect or 
average partial effect
$$\tau(\mathbf z) = \text{Cov}[Y, X | \mathbf Z = \mathbf z] / \text{Var}[X | \mathbf Z = \mathbf z],$$ 
which we interpret as a treatment effect given unconfoundedness. Taking the mean of these treatment
effects over the observed data results in the following interpretation: on average, across all counties in our dataset, a 1\% increase in poverty is associated with a 6.3 increase in CVD deaths per 1000 people.

To account for missingness, we fit the causal forest separately
across $m=5$ multiplly imputed datasets and computed unit-level
conditional average treatment effects (CATEs) for each imputaiton.
These estimates were then averaged across imputations for each county to obtain a stabilized estimate of $\tau(\mathbf z)$.

Like many techniques, estimating the effect of treatment on the treated may be nontrivial in the continuous treatment setting.
Although this may result in some loss of poverty rate variability, we can approximate the effect of treatment on the treated in the traditional sense by discretizing the treatment: we split poverty rate into two groups, designating all rates above the median as "treated", and all rates below the median as "untreated". We then compute the average marginal effect within each group. These results are shown in
Table~\ref{tab:ett}.

We observe that the average marginal effect is slightly higher for 
counties with poverty rates below the median, matching the taper in total effect we observed in Figure~\ref{fig:variation_analysis}.
This suggests that marginal increases in poverty have a stronger effect in relatively lower-poverty counties, while the effect is reduced in already high-poverty regions.
Note that the Average Marginal Effect is more analogous to the local slope of the dose-response curve than the actual outcome level itself. We can also interpret this as evidence of diminishing marginal impact of poverty: once poverty is already widespread, further increases may have a smaller incremental effect on 
CVD mortality.

We can also observe $\mathbf z- TE$'s, that is the average partial effect evaluated at
specific levels of $\mathbf Z$. We conduct this analysis for all of our covariates. For 
the continuous covariates, we partition observations into deciles and compute the mean 
estimated treatment effect within each bin, along with corresponding uncertainty bands 
based on standard errors. Figure~\ref{fig:z_te} shows the results of these investigations.

Starting with the proportion of non-White 
residents, we find
that the trend is relatively flat across most deciles but exhibits a sharp decline int the
highest deciles (above approximately the 7th or 8th decile). This indicates that in 
counties with very large non-White populations, the marginal effect of poverty on CVD 
mortality is substantially reduced.
In these counties, structural and systemic factors may already elevate CVD mortality risk across the population, such that additional increases in poverty have a diminished marginal impact. In this sense, poverty becomes less predictive of variation in outcomes, not because it is unimportant, but because risk is already uniformly high.

The decile of the proportion of the county aged 65 and above exhibits a largely increasing
relationship with $\mathbf z - TE$ up to the upper deciles, followed by a decline.
 This is a highly interpretable result. Younger populations have lower baseline risk of CVD, so poverty has a relatively limited marginal impact. As the population ages,
 vulnerability to CVD increases, and poverty-related factors such as reduced access to care, stress, and 
poor nutrition can exacerbate this risk. Finally, the drop-off at the highest deciles
matches the loss in poverty's effect we would expect as a person 
reaches an age where CVD risk becomes high regardless of socioeconomic status.

The urban-rural classification scheme has a clearly positive
association with $\mathbf z - TE$. As a reminder, higher
values correspond to more rural counties. In Section~\ref{causal_model}, we 
hypothesized that rural
counties would have higher CVD mortality due to reduced access to healthcare. Here, we find a stronger result: not only are outcomes worse, but the effect of poverty itself is amplified in rural settings. This likely reflects structural inequalities such as transportaion barriers, provider shortages, and limited healthcare infrastructure, which make the consequences of poverty more pronounced. 

Lastly, we observe that counties that have expanded Medicaid exhibit lower $\mathbf z-TE$ than those that have not. This suggest that Medicaid expansion may act as a buffer agains the adverse health effects of poverty. By increasing access to healthcare among low-income populations, Medicaid appears to lessen the otherwise strong positive relationship between poverty and CVD mortality. 

Overall, these heterogeneity results highlight that the effect of poverty is not uniform across counties, but instead varies systematically with demographic composition, age structure, geographic context, and policy environment. This reinforces the importance of moving beyond average treatment effects and considering localized, policy-relevant effect variation.

\section{Discussion}
\label{discussion}

Throughout this investigation, we have attacked the causal relationship between poverty and CVD mortality from different angles while considering how covariates come into play.
A key limitation of this study is the reliance on a parametric model for interval-censored outcomes. While the suppression mechanism is known, recovery of the full outcome distribution depends on modeling assumptions. Future work could explore partial identification approaches that provide bounds on causal effects without requiring full distributional assumptions.

This analysis has prompted many future directions to study. It remains an interesting question to decompose the
total causal effect into the natural direct effect and
the natural indirect effect through the mediating pathway
of uninsurance rate. This could either be investigated by
binarizing poverty rate and using mediation analysis methods developed by \citet{farbmacher2022causal} or by taking the non-trivial route and non-parametric method for doubly robust
estimation of mediation effects a la \citet{kennedy2017non}.
