\begin{abstract}
We study the causal effect of county-level poverty rates on cardiovascular 
disease (CVD) mortality in the United States using continuous-treatment causal 
inference methods. Estimating this relationship is complicated by confounding, 
potential nonlinearity, and ensored outcomes resulting from CDC 
suppression rules. We address these challenges by combining doubly robust 
continuous treatment effect estimation with a left-censored multiple 
imputation strategy designed for similar suppression mechanisms. Using 2016 
county-level data, we estimate the causal dose-response function and explore 
heterogeneous effects with causal forests. We find that poverty's causal effect 
on CVD mortality is strongly linear. Furthermore, our heterogeneity 
analysis unexpectedly reveals that the marginal impact of poverty is steeper 
in states with Medicaid expansion, highlighting that the interactions between 
baseline healthcare access and systemic socioeconomic disparities are complex.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

Cardiovascular disease (CVD) remains the leading cause of mortality in the United 
States, representing a profound and persistent public health crisis \citep{tsao2023heart}. 
While clinical treatments have advanced significantly throughout history, the burden of CVD is not 
distributed equally across the American population. A massive body of epidemiological literature 
has established socioeconomic status—and systemic poverty in particular—as a 
fundamental link to this disease \citep{link1995social, marmot2005social}. However, 
understanding the exact, continuous causal relationship between poverty and CVD 
mortality at the population level is notoriously difficult. Observational health 
data is deeply confounded by the intersection of demographic, geographic, and policy-level 
realities \citep{galea2011estimated}.

In this study, we rigorously investigate the continuous causal dose-response 
relationship between county-level poverty rates ($X$) and age-adjusted CVD 
mortality per 100,000 people ($Y$). To isolate the true effect of poverty, we 
must account for a complex network of county-level confounders. Our analysis adjusts 
for the proportion of residents aged 65 and above ($A$), the proportion of the 
non-Hispanic White population ($C$), the local uninsured rate ($M$), state-level 
Medicaid expansion status ($G$), and the National Center for Health Statistics 
(NCHS) urban-rural classification scheme ($R$) \citep{ingram20142013}.

While there is much literature that connects poverty to poor health outcomes, very few studies 
attempt to estimate continuous causal effects at the county level while adequately 
addressing non-random suppression in the data. Traditionally, researchers have relied on 
assumptions that make analysis more convenient. Binarizing the poverty rate (e.g., simply 
comparing counties ``above" or ``below" the national median) destroys meaningful 
continuous variation and relies on arbitrary thresholds. Conversely, standard 
partially linear models \citep{chernozhukov2018double} impose rigid, parametric 
functional forms that may force a linear relationship where a complex curve actually 
exists. Instead, we utilize a highly flexible, non-parametric doubly robust 
estimator \citep{kennedy2017non} designed for continuous treatments, allowing 
us to verify that the dose-response curve is, in fact, linear. In addition, we can visualize a more flexible total variation curve, leading to inferences about the spurious effect.


To explore how the causal effect of poverty varies across distinct subpopulations 
and policy environments, we employ causal forests \citep{wager2018estimation, athey2019generalized}. 
This machine learning approach allows us to discover highly specific, multi-dimensional 
heterogeneity, providing actionable insights into where poverty is most actively 
driving mortality.

An additional challenge in county-level epidemiological research is data 
censoring. To protect patient privacy, the Centers for Disease Control and 
Prevention (CDC) completely suppresses mortality data in counties with low underlying 
death counts (fewer than 10 deaths). Because low death counts are highly correlated 
with small populations, this missingness is decidedly non-random. Ignoring 
these suppressed counties can introduce severe selection bias. To resolve this, we use a Tobit-based 
multiple imputation strategy designed for interval-censored mortality outcomes.

In summary, our main contributions to the literature are: (1) estimating 
a continuous, unconstrained causal dose-response function for poverty using more recent
doubly robust machine learning methods; (2) utilizing a
Tobit-based multiple imputation strategy to recover interval-censored public health 
data; and (3) conducting a  heterogeneity analysis that links 
state policy and local demographics to the localized variation in poverty's causal effect.

The paper proceeds as follows. Section~\ref{causal_model} outlines our structural 
causal assumptions and directed acyclic graph, while Section~\ref{data} details 
the construction of the dataset and the specific missingness scheme. Section~\ref{sensitivity} 
validates our multiple imputation strategy. We then formally define the causal 
quantities of interest in Section~\ref{quantities}, describe the doubly robust 
estimation procedure and its confidence bands calculations in Section~\ref{estimation}, 
present the sub-population heterogeneity analysis in Section~\ref{heterogeneity}, 
and conclude with a discussion of our findings and their potential policy implications 
in Section~\ref{discussion}.

The structural edges in our model are grounded in both sociological 
theory and epidemiology. Poverty ($X$) directly increases CVD 
mortality ($Y$) through physiological pathways—such as allostatic load, chronic 
inflammation from sustained stress, and poor nutritional environments 
\citep{kim2025mediating}. Simultaneously, poverty indirectly reduces healthcare 
access by pricing individuals out of coverage \citep{khatana2019association}. 
Medicaid expansion ($G$) structurally 
reducing uninsurance ($M$) and lowers overall CVD mortality, with 
\citet{miller2021medicaid} noting a robust 9.4\% reduction in annual mortality 
linked to the policy. 

Demographic and geographic confounders exert deeply interconnected effects. Racial 
composition ($C$) increases county-level poverty and uninsurance through a long history of redlining and structural racism \citep{bailey2021structural, lee2021convergence}. 
It also drives CVD mortality independently through biological ``weathering", which hypothesizes 
that marginalized populations—particularly Black Americans—experience premature 
biological aging and earlier onset of cardiovascular events due to cumulative 
psychological stress \citep{geronimus2006weathering}. Rurality ($R$), largely 
defined by population size ($P$), is associated with fewer labor markets, therefore 
raising poverty \citep{farrigan2026rural}. Rurality actively increases 
CVD mortality due to the disparities in nutrition between urban and rural areas \citep{loccoh2022rural}. Finally, age ($A$) acts as an 
unavoidable biological driver of CVD mortality and serves as a vital precision variable. 
Bidirected edges account for spatial demographic correlations; for instance, 
racial minorities comprise 22\% of the rural and 43\% of the urban populations 
\citep{castillo2020racial}, and rural counties consistently trend older as 
younger populations are more likely to migrate to urban centers \citep{monnat2025us, cohen2023aging}.

Our model incorporates the missingness mechanism for CVD 
mortality. According to CDC WONDER privacy documentation \citep{cdc_wonder_cmf_doc}, 
mortality data suppression is deterministic: a county's rate is censored 
($R_Y = 1$) if the total recorded deaths $D < 10$. Because total deaths are a
mathematical product of population size ($P$), age structure ($A$), and the true 
underlying adjusted mortality rate ($Y$), the suppression rule takes 
the form $R_Y \leftarrow \mathds{1}\{D < 10\}$. Because the missingness of $Y$ is 
directly caused by the unobserved value of $Y$ itself, this is an example of
Missing Not At Random (MNAR) mechanism, illustrated by the red paths in Figure~\ref{fig:dag}. 
We assume population size ($P$) only affects the model through defining urbanicity 
and mechanically triggering suppression. As a result, traditional complete-case 
analysis (dropping censored counties) could induce large selection bias by 
systematically deleting the healthiest small counties, which could heavily distort our conclusions.




\section{Data}
\label{data}

We compile 2016 county-level data from multiple public sources, 
merging by FIPS code to create a unified dataset. Our primary 
outcome, age-adjusted CVD mortality per 100,000 people ($Y$), is sourced from 
CDC WONDER \citep{cdc_wonder_cmf_data}. Age adjustment standardizes the outcome 
to the year-2000 national reference population, removing the inherent effect 
of a county's raw age structure \citep{anderson1998age}. For our treatment and 
demographic covariates, we utilize the American Community Survey (ACS) 5-year 
estimates (2012-2016) for the poverty rate ($X$), non-white population percentage 
($C$), and percentage aged 65 and older ($A$). The 5-year ACS estimates are 
specifically chosen over 1-year estimates because the latter do not provide data 
for geographies with populations under 65,000, which would artificially truncate 
smaller counties. We retain $A$ as a covariate in our models because 
standardizing to a national reference population may not perfectly eliminate 
residual age confounding in counties with extreme or highly skewed demographic profiles.

Uninsurance rates ($M$) are drawn from the Small Area Health Insurance Estimates 
(SAHIE), and state-level Medicaid expansion status ($G$) as of January 1, 2016, is 
obtained from the Kaiser Family Foundation (KFF). Finally, we adopt the 2013 
National Center for Health Statistics (NCHS) urban-rural classification ($R$) 
\citep{ingram20142013}. This six-level ordinal scheme ranges from large central 
metropolitan areas (level 1) to non-core, completely rural counties (level 6). 
This scheme was deliberately chosen over raw population density because it better 
captures functional economic integration, commuting patterns, and realized 
healthcare access zones \citep{office20212020}.

After reconciling census area variations (e.g., standardizing unincorporated Alaskan 
boroughs and independent Virginian cities), our baseline observational sample 
comprises 3,142 U.S. counties and county-equivalents. Six counties were 
excluded due to missing data not due to suppression. An 
additional 168 counties had their precise CVD mortality rates suppressed due to 
experiencing fewer than 10 recorded cardiovascular deaths in the 2016.

As established in Section~\ref{causal_model}, this deterministic 
suppression results in an MNAR problem. Because the true outcome $Y$ 
determines its own missingness $R_Y$, the joint distribution $\mathbb{P}(\mathbf{V})$ 
is not non-parametrically identifiable from the observed data alone. However, the 
exact nature of the CDC suppression rule ($D < 10$) allows us 
to reframe this missingness as aninterval-censoring problem. 
For every suppressed county, the unobserved true mortality rate is  bounded 
between 0 and a known specific Limit of Detection (LOD). We calculate 
this as $\text{LOD}_i = 900,000 / P_i$, where $P_i$ is the specific county population, 
effectively bounding the rate just below the 10-death threshold.

We recover the complete joint distribution under parametric modeling assumptions 
using Multiple Imputation by Chained Equations (MICE) paired with a 
left-censored outcome model introduced by \citep{keil2019quantile}. Prior to imputation, counties 
with exactly zero observed deaths (which breaks logarithmic likelihood) were corrected to a nominal rate of $100,000 / P_i$ (representing exactly 1 
theoretical death) for stability purposes. For the 168 suppressed counties, the 
missing outcome values were multiply imputed ($M=5$ datasets, $I=10$ algorithmic 
iterations) using Tobit regression. Each drawn value was 
sampled from a conditional normal distribution that was right-truncated 
at the county's specific $\text{LOD}_i$. The imputation predictor matrix 
included all causal covariates and geographic identifiers, ensuring the stochastically 
drawn distributions respected each county's unique demographic and 
spatial structure. Our final estimation relies on 
this parametric Tobit imputation model's correctness in capturing the true tail-end variance of 
the mortality distribution.

\section{Sensitivity Analysis}
\label{sensitivity}

To empirically demonstrate that our left-censored MICE method provides superior 
estimation performance relative to complete case analysis (CCA) and standard 
multiple imputation, we design and conduct a simulation study. 
Rather than relying on purely theoretical data, we construct 
semi-synthetic data by preserving the exact empirical covariates  of 
the counties in our 2016 dataset. This ensures that the simulated 
confounding and missingness mechanisms somewhat mimic the real-world public 
health data environment.

We simulate an age-adjusted CVD mortality outcome $Y$, the corresponding raw 
(unadjusted) mortality $Y_{\text{raw}}$, and a deterministic missingness 
indicator $R_Y$. Following CDC WONDER documentation \citep{cdc_wonder_cmf_doc}, 
age-adjusted mortality is mathematically defined as a standardized weighted 
sum of age-specific raw mortality rates.

For simplicity while preserving some demographic heterogeneity, we stratify the population into two age groups: individuals younger than 65 years old ($k=1$) 
and individuals aged 65 or older ($k=2$). We define the empirical standard 
population weights as $w_1 = \sum (1-A_i)P_i / \sum P_i$ and 
$w_2 = \sum A_i P_i / \sum P_i$, respectively, where $A_i$ is the county 
proportion of residents 65 and older, and $P_i$ is the total county population

We generate the true raw CVD mortality rates per 100,000 for each age group 
using distinct baseline intercepts and variances to reflect the naturally 
higher risk and volatility in older populations. For the $<65$ cohort, mortality 
is drawn as:
$$
Y_{\text{raw},1} \sim \mathcal{N}(\mu_1, 200^2)
$$
where $\mu_1 = 100 + 1500X + 500C + 400M - 20R - 20G$. For the $\geq65$ cohort, 
mortality is drawn as:
$$
Y_{\text{raw},2} \sim \mathcal{N}(\mu_2, 400^2)
$$
where $\mu_2 = 1000 + 1500X + 500C + 400M - 20R - 20G$. To enforce the 
non-negativity of mortality rates, any negative draws are truncated to zero. In this data generating process, the true causal parameter of interest for 
poverty ($X$) is fixed at $\beta = 1500$.

The overall crude mortality rate $Y_{\text{raw}}$ is then constructed for each 
county as the local population-weighted average of the two groups. The final 
observed age-adjusted outcome $Y$ is computed using the aforementioned weights 
$w_1$ and $w_2$. Finally, to enforce the CDC suppression mechanism, we 
calculate the expected number of total deaths $D = P \cdot Y_{\text{raw}} / 100,000$. 
The suppression indicator is deterministically assigned as 
$R_Y = \mathds{1}\{D < 10\}$. Any county meeting this criterion has its 
outcome $Y$ set to missing.

Over 100 replications, we apply three distinct estimation 
strategies to the semi-synthetic datasets: (1) CCA, 
which simply drops suppressed counties; (2) Standard MICE ($M=5$ imputations); and (3) Left-censored MICE 
($M=5$ imputations) using a Tobit regression model bounded by the deterministic 
Limit of Detection ($\text{LOD}_i = 900,000 / P_i$). For each method, we extract 
the coefficient for poverty ($X$) by fitting ordinary least squares and we calculate the 
estimation bias as $\hat{\beta} - 1500$.

The distribution of estimation bias across the 100 replications is presented 
in Figure~\ref{fig:sensitivity}. The results clearly illustrate the potential practical failure that could result from using standard approaches. CCA suffers from severe selection bias 
because it is forced to drops low-population rural counties, which could significantly alter the covariate distribution. 
Standard MICE also exhibits substantial bias. Because it does not use our knowledge about the suppression mechanism, it uses observed high-mortality counties to impute 
values for suppressed counties, routinely exceeding the ceiling set by the CDC.

In contrast, the left-censored Tobit MICE approach incorporates 
the $D < 10$ bound, forcing the imputation algorithm to draw from the 
appropriate truncated tail of the distribution. While a noticeable amount of bias still remains, the Tobit specification does result in a decrease
in bias and centers the estimate closer to the true parameter, 
justifying its use in our primary empirical analysis.

\section{Causal Quantity of Interest}
\label{quantities}

In this section, we formally define the causal methods used to analyze the 
effect of poverty rates on CVD mortality. Because both our treatment ($X$, 
poverty rate) and outcome ($Y$, mortality rate) are continuous, our analysis 
is different from standard binary potential outcome frameworks. Rather than 
comparing two level (e.g., $x_1$ versus $x_0$), we aim to estimate 
the entire continuous dose-response function. This allows us to evaluate the 
marginal effect of an incremental shift $\delta$ at any arbitrary baseline 
poverty level $x_0$, such that $x_1 = x_0 + \delta$.

Let $Y_x$ denote the potential outcome—the age-adjusted CVD mortality rate 
that would be observed for a county if its poverty rate were hypothetically 
set to $x$ via an external intervention, holding all else constant. Our 
primary causal quantity
represents the Total Effect ($TE$) of poverty across the support of $X$:
$$TE(x) = \mathbb{E}[Y_x]$$
Here, $\mathbb{E}[Y_x]$ is the expected CVD mortality if a county 
is set to poverty rate $x$ over the distribution of the covariates.

To quantify the magnitude of confounding, we contrast this causal curve with 
the observational Total Variation ($TV$) curve, which is simply the conditional 
expectation of the outcome given the observed treatment:
$$TV(x) = \mathbb{E}[Y \mid X = x]$$
The pointwise difference between the observational curve and the causal curve 
isolates the Spurious Effect (SE), which captures the
association driven by confounding variables rather than a true causal 
pathway. We define the Spurious Effect as:
$$SE(x) = TV(x) - TE(x) = \mathbb{E}[Y \mid X = x] - \mathbb{E}[Y_x]$$
By estimating both $TV(x)$ and $TE(x)$, we can inherently recover $SE(x)$ by 
calculating the vertical distance between the two curves across $X$.

While decomposing the Total Effect into Natural Direct Effects (NDE) and 
Natural Indirect Effects (NIE) through the uninsurance pathway ($M$) is of 
theoretical interest, we focus on the Total Effect in this paper. 
Because accounting for mediation is non-trivial in this setting and remains a larger problem unaddressed in the literature, we leave the question as room for future work.

To non-parametrically identify the continuous Total Effect $TE(x)$ from 
observational data, we invoke the following three standard assumptions 
extended for continuous treatments as in \citep{kennedy2017non}. Let $\mathcal{X}$ 
and $\mathcal{Z}$ be the respective support spaces of the treatment and 
covariates.
 
\begin{assumption}[Consistency]
$X=x \rightarrow Y = Y_x$
\end{assumption}

\begin{assumption}[Positivity]
$\pi(x|\mathbf z) \geq \pi_{min} > 0,\forall \ \mathbf z \in \mathcal Z$
\end{assumption}

\begin{assumption}[Ignorability]
$\mathbb E[Y_x|\mathbf Z,X] = \mathbb E[Y_x|\mathbf Z]$
\end{assumption}

Based on the Directed Acyclic Graph (DAG) presented in 
Section~\ref{causal_model}, we construct our adjustment set $\mathbf{Z}$ to 
satisfy the backdoor criterion. The minimal sufficient adjustment set 
to block all backdoor paths is $\{C, R\}$, which severs the confounding 
influence of racial composition ($X \leftarrow C \rightarrow Y$ and 
$X \leftarrow C \rightarrow M \rightarrow Y$) and rurality 
($X \leftarrow R \rightarrow Y$ and $X \leftarrow R \rightarrow M \rightarrow Y$).

However, to improve the statistical efficiency of our continuous estimator, 
we utilize an expanded adjustment set: $\mathbf{Z} = \{C, R, A, G\}$. By 
including the proportion of the population over 65 ($A$) and state Medicaid 
expansion status ($G$) as precision variables, we aim to reduce residual variance in the outcome $Y$, which can tighten our confidence bands without 
adding bias. We exclude the uninsurance rate ($M$) 
from $\mathbf{Z}$. Because $M$ lies on the causal pathway between $X$ and 
$Y$, conditioning on it would block the 
indirect channels that comprise the Total Effect.

Under these assumptions, the continuous causal effect is identified using 
backdoor adjustment, integrating over the marginal distribution of the 
covariates $P(\mathbf{z})$:
$$\mathbb{E}[Y_x] = \int_{\mathcal{Z}} \mathbb{E}[Y \mid X = x, \mathbf{Z} = \mathbf{z}] dP(\mathbf{z})$$

\section{Estimation of Causal Effect}
\label{estimation}


An estimate of the total variation $\widehat{TV}(x)$ can be obtained
with naive non-parametric regression on $X$ alone, unadjusted for any 
other covariates. In our application, we use local linear regression 
with a Gaussian kernel. The optimal bandwidth $h_{TV}^*$ is selected 
empirically by minimizing the leave-one-out cross-validation score:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:cv}
    CV(h) = \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n\Big(\frac{Y_i-\hat{Y}_i(h)}{1-h_{ii}}\Big)^2,
\end{equation}
where $h_{ii}$ is the $i$-th diagonal element of the hat matrix.

Estimating the total effect $\widehat{TE}(x)$ in the continuous treatment 
setting is substantially more complex and necessitates advanced doubly 
robust tools. We utilize the continuous treatment framework developed by 
\citet{kennedy2017non}. Let $\mu(\mathbf{z}, x) = \mathbb{E}[Y \mid \mathbf{Z} = \mathbf{z}, X = x]$ 
denote the conditional expectation of the outcome. We can rewrite the 
causal effect as the marginalized outcome over the covariate space:
$$\mathbb{E}[Y_x] = \int_{\mathcal{Z}} \mu(\mathbf{z}, x) dP(\mathbf{z})$$

Furthermore, let $\bar{\omega}(x) = \partial \mathbb{P}(X \leq x)/\partial x$ 
denote the marginal treatment density, and $\pi(x \mid \mathbf{z}) = \partial \mathbb{P}(X \leq x \mid \mathbf{Z} = \mathbf{z}) / \partial x$ 
denote the conditional treatment density. 
The objective is to construct a doubly robust function $\xi(\mathbf{V}; \pi, \mu)$ 
of the observed data $\mathbf{V}$ and nuisance functions $(\pi, \mu)$ such that 
its expectation equals causal parameter $\psi$, given that at least one 
nuisance function is consistently estimated.

\begin{theorem}[Efficient Influence Function \citep{kennedy2017non}]
    Under a non-parametric model, the efficient influence function for 
    $\psi = \int_{\mathcal{X}} \int_{\mathcal{Z}} \mu(\mathbf{z}, x) \bar{\omega}(x) dP(\mathbf{z}) dx$ 
    is defined as $\xi(\mathbf{V}; \pi, \mu) - \psi + \int_{\mathcal{X}} \{ \mu(\mathbf{Z}, x) - \int_{\mathcal{Z}} \mu(\mathbf{z}, x) dP(\mathbf{z}) \} \bar{\omega}(x) dx$, 
    where the pseudo-outcome is:
    \begin{multline*}
        \xi(\mathbf{V}; \pi, \mu) = \frac{Y - \mu(\mathbf{Z}, X)}{\pi(X \mid \mathbf{Z})} \int_{\mathcal{Z}} \pi(X \mid \mathbf{z}) dP(\mathbf{z}) \\
        + \int_{\mathcal{Z}} \mu(\mathbf{z}, X) dP(\mathbf{z})
    \end{multline*}
\end{theorem}

To construct the empirical pseudo-outcome, we must first estimate the nuisance 
functions $\hat{\mu}$ and $\hat{\pi}$. For the conditional expectation 
$\hat{\mu}(\mathbf{Z}, X)$, we utilize the \texttt{SuperLearner} ensemble 
algorithm \citep{SuperLearner} with 10-fold cross-validation, employing 
XGBoost base learners. As detailed in \citet{cdc_wonder_cmf_doc}, rates derived from 
low underlying death counts are highly variable. To protect our estimators 
against this heteroskedasticity, we weight the objective functions 
of all machine learning models by the total county population $P_i$.

Estimating the conditional density $\hat{\pi}(x \mid \mathbf{z})$ can be quite difficult in continuous settings. We break this into a two-stage 
location-scale model. First, we regress $X$ on $\mathbf{Z}$ using a SuperLearner 
to obtain the conditional mean $\hat{\mu}_X(\mathbf{Z}) = \mathbb{E}[X \mid \mathbf{Z}]$ 
and extract the residuals $\hat{\epsilon} = X - \hat{\mu}_X(\mathbf{Z})$. 
Second, we regress the squared residuals on $\mathbf{Z}$ to estimate the 
conditional variance $\hat{\sigma}^2_X(\mathbf{Z}) = \max(\mathbb{E}[\hat{\epsilon}^2 \mid \mathbf{Z}], 10^{-4})$, 
where the lower bound is enforced to prevent numerical explosion in the density 
ratio.

Rather than relying on strict Gaussian density assumptions, we estimate the 
conditional density non-parametrically. We standardize the treatment residuals 
as $\tilde{\epsilon}_i = \hat{\epsilon}_i / \hat{\sigma}_X(\mathbf{Z}_i)$ 
and apply Kernel Density Estimation (KDE). An estimate of the conditional density evaluated 
at each county's observed treatment is obtained with cubic smoothing splines 
evaluated over the KDE space.

Using empirical measures $\mathbb{P}_n$, we generate counterfactual matrices 
evaluating $\hat{\mu}$ and $\hat{\pi}$ for all $n$ counties across a fixed 
grid $\mathcal{X}_{\text{eval}}$. Taking the column means is equivalent to integrating out the 
covariates over the empirical measure, giving us our marginal quantities. The final empirical 
pseudo-outcome is constructed as:
\begin{multline}
\label{eq:pseudo}
    \hat{\xi}(\mathbf{V}; \hat{\pi}, \hat{\mu}) = \frac{Y - \hat{\mu}(\mathbf{Z}, X)}{\hat{\pi}(X \mid \mathbf{Z})} \int_{\mathcal{Z}} \hat{\pi}(X \mid \mathbf{z}) d\mathbb{P}_n(\mathbf{z}) \\
    + \int_{\mathcal{Z}} \hat{\mu}(\mathbf{z}, X) d\mathbb{P}_n(\mathbf{z})
\end{multline}
We then regress $\hat{\xi}$ on $X$ using local linear regression with an 
internally optimized cross-validated bandwidth $h_{TE}^*$ to produce the 
final continuous causal curve $\widehat{TE}(x)$.

To quantify uncertainty (again following \citet{kennedy2017non}), we compute a sandwich variance estimator 
directly from the empirical score equations of the local linear regressions. 
For a given evaluation point $x$, let $\mathbf{D}_h$ represent the empirical 
design matrix of the kernel weights, and let $\mathbf{S}_i$ denote the 
estimated influence function components for the $i$-th county, which includes 
the regression score altered by the numerical integral of the nuisance 
corrections. The pointwise variance for the $m$-th imputed dataset is 
calculated as $\hat{\Sigma}^{(m)}(x) = \text{Cov}(\mathbf{D}_h^{-1} \mathbf{S})$.

The entire procedure is executed identically across all $M=5$ left-censored 
imputed datasets, and we then aggregate the pointwise curve estimates using Rubin's 
Rules \citep{rubin1987multiple}. The pooled point estimate is 
$\widehat{TE}(x) = \frac{1}{M}\sum_{m=1}^M \widehat{TE}^{(m)}(x)$. 
The total pointwise variance is composed of the average 
within-imputation variance ($W$) and the between-imputation variance ($B$):
$$V_{TE}(x) = W_{TE}(x) + \left(1 + \frac{1}{M}\right)B_{TE}(x)$$
The final 95\% confidence bands at each evaluation point are constructed as 
$\widehat{TE}(x) \pm 1.96 \sqrt{V_{TE}(x)}$.

For the sake of comparison, we simultaneously calculate the 
pointwise variance for the unadjusted total variation ($\widehat{TV}$). This 
procedure is similar to the $\widehat{TE}$ variance estimation, pooling the 
unadjusted local linear regressions across the $M=5$ imputations using the same 
application of Rubin's Rules.
Because the total variation is a naive regression of $Y$ on $X$, its residual 
error inherently absorbs all the unadjusted variance driven by the complex demographics 
in $\mathbf{Z}$. Consequently, as visually evident in our results, the confidence 
bands for the observational total variation ($\widehat{TV}$) are naturally wider 
than those of the doubly robust total effect ($\widehat{TE}$), especially at extreme 
high values of CVD mortality where the structural variance of the outcome is inherently larger.

\section{Causal Effect Heterogeneity}
\label{heterogeneity}
To offer an alternative methodological approach and to enable ananalysis of heterogeneity in the causal effect, we applied a 
continuous-treatment causal forest using the \texttt{grf} package \citep{grf}. 

When the treatment is continuous, the \texttt{grf} algorithm estimates the 
conditional Average Partial Effect (APE), defined as the localized ratio of 
covariance to variance:
$$\tau(\mathbf{z}) = \frac{\text{Cov}[Y, X \mid \mathbf{Z} = \mathbf{z}]}{\text{Var}[X \mid \mathbf{Z} = \mathbf{z}]}$$
Under the assumption of unconfoundedness, this represents the individualized 
treatment effect given a specific covariate profile $\mathbf{z}$. Taking the mean of these individualized treatment effects over our 
observed data yields the following global interpretation: on average, across 
all counties in our dataset, a 1\% increase in poverty is associated with a 
5.06 increase in age-adjusted CVD deaths per 100,000 people.

To rigorously account for the uncertainty introduced by missing data, we fit 
separate causal forests across the $m=5$ multiply imputed datasets. For each 
imputation, we computed the unit-level conditional average treatment effects 
(CATEs). These estimates were then aggregated across the imputations for each 
county to obtain a stabilized, pooled estimate of $\tau(\mathbf{z})$.

Like many non-parametric techniques, isolating the specific effect of treatment 
on the treated (ETT) can be non-trivial in a continuous treatment regime. 
Although dichotomization naturally results in some loss of continuous variance, 
we can approximate the traditional ETT by discretizing the poverty rate. We 
split the poverty rate into two distinct regimes, designating all rates above 
the national median as ``High Poverty" (treated) and all rates below the median 
as ``Low Poverty" (untreated). We then compute the Average Marginal Effect (AME) 
within each group. These results are detailed in Table~\ref{tab:ett}.

We observe that the Average Marginal Effect is slightly higher for counties 
with poverty rates below the median. While our macroscopic analysis in 
Section~\ref{estimation} revealed a broadly linear global dose-response, 
this localized forest analysis suggests a subtle variation in the slope: 
marginal increases in poverty have a slightly steeper impact in relatively 
lower-poverty counties. Conversely, the incremental effect is slightly reduced 
in already high-poverty regions. Because the AME is analogous to the local 
derivative of the dose-response curve rather than the absolute outcome level, 
this suggests a mild diminishing marginal impact: once systemic poverty is 
already widespread and baseline mortality is high, further incremental shifts 
in poverty yield a marginally smaller (though still positive) impact on CVD 
mortality.

\input{manuscript/tables/ett}

We further investigate the $\mathbf{z}-TE$s—the average partial effect 
evaluated at specific stratifications of $\mathbf{Z}$. We conduct this 
analysis for all four of our primary confounders and precision variables. For 
continuous covariates, we partition observations into deciles and compute the 
mean estimated treatment effect within each bin, applying corresponding 
uncertainty bands based on standard errors. Figure~\ref{fig:z_te} presents 
the results of these investigations.


Starting with the proportion of non-White 
residents, we observe an initial increase in the treatment effect across the 
lower and middle deciles, followed by a sharp decline in the highest deciles (above 
approximately the 7th decile). The initial upward trend suggests a 
compounding vulnerability: as the minority population grows from low to 
moderate levels, the intersection of racial inequities and economic 
disenfranchisement compounds, increasing the marginal lethality of poverty. However, in the highest deciles—representing hyper-segregated or 
majority-minority counties—the marginal effect of poverty substantially drops. 
Sociologically, this implies that systemic and structural racism may elevate 
the baseline CVD mortality risk so severely across the board that additional 
fluctuations in economic poverty have a diminished incremental impact.

The decile of the proportion of the county aged 65 
and above exhibits an initially increasing relationship with the $\mathbf{z}-TE$, 
followed by a highly pronounced and steep decrease at the upper deciles. 
Younger populations possess a lower inherent physiological risk of CVD, rendering 
the socio-environmental impacts of poverty relatively limited. As the population 
begins to age, poverty-related stressors—such as housing instability, poor 
nutrition, and uninsurance—severely exacerbate developing CVD risks. However, the pronounced 
drop-off at the extreme highest deciles perfectly illustrates a biological 
ceiling, where in the most elderly counties, advanced age becomes the 
overwhelming, dominant driver of cardiovascular mortality. Once a population 
crosses this biological threshold, the marginal predictive power of socioeconomic 
status sharply diminishes.

The urban-rural classification scheme demonstrates a 
mainly positive macro-association with the $\mathbf{z}-TE$, though with notable 
non-linear variations. Specifically, we observe a slight spike at category 2 
(large fringe metropolitan/suburban counties), followed by a localized decrease 
between categories 5 and 6 (micropolitan to non-core transitions). The overall positive trend validates our 
hypothesis that spatial inequalities, such as severe provider shortages and 
transportation barriers, amplify the effect of poverty in rural 
settings. The localized spike at category 2 may reflect the unique phenomenon 
of suburban poverty, where low-income populations are isolated from the dense 
public safety nets and transit systems traditionally found in urban cores (category 1). 
Despite the slight fluctuation in the middle categories, the overarching reality 
remains clear: poverty is a stronger driver of CVD mortality as counties become 
more isolated.

Lastly, and somewhat counterintuitively, we observe 
that counties located in states that have expanded Medicaid exhibit a higher 
$\mathbf{z}$-TE than those in non-expansion states. While one might initially 
assume expansion would flatten the effect of poverty, we hypothesize that this 
dynamic could indicate a shifting baseline in mortality risk. In non-expansion 
states, broadly restricted healthcare access might lead to elevated CVD mortality 
across a wider socioeconomic spectrum, potentially flattening the local slope if 
even moderate-income populations face significant access barriers. Conversely, if 
Medicaid expansion successfully lowers the baseline mortality floor by protecting 
the near-poor and working class, the remaining CVD mortality burden may become 
more heavily concentrated among the deeply impoverished who still face complex 
systemic barriers. Consequently, it is possible that while overall population 
outcomes improve in expansion states, extreme poverty remains a steeply predictive 
factor for cardiovascular death.

Overall, these heterogeneity results highlight that the effect of poverty is not uniform across counties, but instead varies systematically with demographic composition, age structure, geographic context, and policy environment. This reinforces the importance of moving beyond average treatment effects and considering localized, policy-relevant effect variation.

\section{Discussion}
\label{discussion}

Throughout this investigation, we have examined the complex causal relationship 
between poverty and cardiovascular disease (CVD) mortality from multiple 
methodological angles, carefully isolating the total effect from underlying 
demographic and geographic confounders. Our analysis using doubly 
robust estimation revealed a stark, linear dose-response relationship: as 
county-level poverty increases, age-adjusted CVD mortality strictly and 
consistently increases. Furthermore, by contrasting this causal curve with a 
naive observational estimate, we demonstrated that raw demographic variation 
heavily masks the true, steeper penalty of poverty. Our localized causal forest 
analysis added crucial nuance to this reality, proving that the marginal lethality of poverty is significantly amplified by 
advanced age, rural isolation, and a lack of state-level healthcare safety nets.

Despite the robust semi-parametric methods employed for effect estimation, a key 
limitation of this study is our reliance on a parametric model to handle 
interval-censored mortality outcomes. While the Centers for Disease Control and 
Prevention's suppression criterion (censoring death counts below 10) is a 
known quantity, the recovery of the full outcome distribution inherently depends 
on our multiple imputation modeling assumptions. If the true underlying 
distribution of these sparse counts deviates significantly from our specified 
parametric family, our pooled point estimates may carry unrecognized bias at the 
tails. 

Furthermore, as with all observational causal inference, our estimates inherently 
rely on the assumption of strong ignorability or unconfoundedness. While we 
adjusted for a robust set of systemic covariates—including racial composition, 
age structure, rurality, and Medicaid expansion—there remains the persistent 
threat of unmeasured confounding. Unobserved variables such as local nutritional 
environments (e.g., food deserts), localized environmental pollution exposure, 
and behavioral health trends (e.g., smoking and physical activity rates) likely 
intersect with both poverty and CVD risk, potentially biasing our estimates of 
the true causal effect.

This analysis prompts several compelling directions for future epidemiological 
and statistical research. Chief among them is the formal decomposition of the 
total causal effect into the natural direct effect and the natural indirect effect, 
specifically operating through the mediating pathway of uninsurance rates. Our 
heterogeneity findings regarding Medicaid expansion strongly suggest that healthcare 
access actively mitigates the lethality of poverty. Formally estimating this 
mediation could be approached in two ways: either by binarizing the poverty rate 
and applying the robust mediation analysis methods developed by 
\citet{farbmacher2022causal}, or by undertaking the statistically non-trivial 
route of developing non-parametric, doubly robust continuous mediation estimators 
akin to the total effect framework established by \citet{kennedy2017non}.

Ultimately, these findings could carry urgent implications for public health policy. 
By demonstrating that poverty linearly and directly exacerbates CVD mortality, 
and that this effect is severely amplified in rural and non-Medicaid expansion 
contexts, our results argue against uniform, blanket health interventions. Instead, 
policymakers must target the systemic economic root causes of health disparities, 
recognizing that poverty itself is a sharp cardiovascular risk factor that 
requires structural treatment.
