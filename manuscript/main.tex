\begin{abstract}
We study the causal effect of county-level poverty rates on cardiovascular 
disease (CVD) mortality in the United States using continuous-treatment causal 
inference methods. Estimating this relationship is complicated by confounding, 
potential nonlinearity, and censored outcomes resulting from CDC  
suppression rules. We address these challenges by combining doubly robust 
continuous treatment effect estimation with a left-censored multiple 
imputation strategy for suppression mechanisms. Using 2016 
county-level data, we estimate the causal dose-response function and explore 
heterogeneous effects with causal forests. We find a linear causal effect of poverty. Furthermore, our heterogeneity 
analysis reveals that the marginal impact of poverty is steeper 
in states with Medicaid expansion, suggesting complex interactions between healthcare access and socioeconomic disparities.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

Cardiovascular disease (CVD) remains the leading cause of mortality in the United 
States, meaning that it is a major public health problem\citep{tsao2023heart}. 
The burden of CVD is not 
distributed equally across the American population. Many studies have established socioeconomic status—poverty in particular—as a 
fundamental link to this disease \citep{link1995social, marmot2005social}. However, 
estimating the continuous causal relationship between poverty and CVD 
mortality at the population level is difficult. Observational health 
data is confounded by demographic, geographic, and policy-level 
factors \citep{galea2011estimated}.

In this study, we investigate the continuous causal dose-response 
relationship between county-level poverty rates ($X$) and age-adjusted CVD 
mortality per 100,000 people ($Y$). To isolate the effect of poverty, we 
must account for county-level confounders. Our analysis adjusts 
for the proportion of residents aged 65 and above ($A$), the proportion of the 
non-Hispanic White population ($C$), the local uninsured rate ($M$), state-level 
Medicaid expansion status ($G$), and the National Center for Health Statistics 
(NCHS) urban-rural classification scheme ($R$) \citep{ingram20142013}.

While the aforementioned literature connects poverty to poor health outcomes, few studies 
attempt to estimate continuous causal effects at the county level while adequately 
addressing non-random suppression in the data. Researchers have implicitly relied on 
assumptions that make analysis more convenient. Binarizing the poverty rate (or 
comparing counties ``above" or ``below" the national median) removes 
continuous variation and relies on arbitrary thresholds. An alternative method,
partially linear models \citep{chernozhukov2018double}, impose rigid, parametric 
functional forms that force a linear relationship where a more complex curve may
exist in reality. Instead, we utilize a more flexible, non-parametric doubly robust 
estimator \citep{kennedy2017non} designed for continuous treatments, allowing 
us to verify that the dose-response curve is, in fact, linear. In addition, we can visualize a more flexible total variation curve, which also helps us make inferences about the spurious effect.


To explore how the causal effect of poverty varies across subpopulations 
and policy environments, we apply causal forests \citep{wager2018estimation, athey2019generalized}. 
This machine learning approach allows us to discover more specific, multi-dimensional 
heterogeneity across covariate levels, identifying where poverty has a stronger effect.

A key challenge is data 
censoring. The Centers for Disease Control and 
Prevention (CDC) suppresses mortality data in counties with low death counts (fewer than 10 deaths). Because low death counts are correlated 
with small populations, this missingness is non-random. Ignoring 
these suppressed counties can introduce selection bias. To resolve this, we use a Tobit-based 
multiple imputation strategy designed for interval-censored mortality outcomes.

In summary, our main contributions are: (1) estimating 
a continuous, unconstrained causal dose-response function for poverty using more recent
doubly robust machine learning methods; (2) utilizing a
Tobit-based multiple imputation strategy to recover interval-censored public health 
data; and (3) conducting a  heterogeneity analysis that links 
state policy and local demographics to the localized variation in poverty's causal effect.

The paper proceeds as follows. Section~\ref{causal_model} outlines our structural 
causal assumptions and directed acyclic graph, while Section~\ref{data} details 
the construction of the dataset and the specific missingness scheme. Section~\ref{sensitivity} 
validates our multiple imputation strategy. We then formally define the causal 
quantities of interest in Section~\ref{quantities}, describe the doubly robust 
estimation procedure and its confidence bands calculations in Section~\ref{estimation}, 
present the sub-population heterogeneity analysis in Section~\ref{heterogeneity}, 
and conclude with a discussion of our findings and their potential policy implications 
in Section~\ref{discussion}.

The structural edges in our model are grounded in both sociological 
theory and epidemiology. In our model, poverty ($X$)  increases CVD 
mortality ($Y$) through physiological pathways—such as allostatic load, chronic 
inflammation from sustained stress, and poor nutritional environments 
\citep{kim2025mediating}. We include these in the direct pathway from $X$ to $Y$. Simultaneously, poverty reduces healthcare 
access by pricing individuals out of coverage \citep{khatana2019association}. We represent this as the indirect pathway from $X$ to $M$ to $Y$.
Medicaid expansion ($G$) is associated with a 
reduction uninsurance ($M$) and lowers CVD mortality, with 
\citet{miller2021medicaid} noting a significant 9.4\% reduction in annual mortality 
linked to the policy. 

There are also a few demographic and geographic confounders. Racial 
composition ($C$) increases county-level poverty and uninsurance through a long history of redlining and structural racism \citep{bailey2021structural, lee2021convergence}. 
It also drives CVD mortality independently through biological ``weathering", which hypothesizes 
that marginalized populations—particularly Black Americans—experience premature 
biological aging and earlier onset of cardiovascular events due to cumulative 
psychological stress \citep{geronimus2006weathering}. Rurality ($R$) is associated with fewer labor markets, therefore 
raising poverty \citep{farrigan2026rural}. Rurality can also increase
CVD mortality due to the disparities in nutrition between urban and rural areas \citep{loccoh2022rural}. Finally, age ($A$) naturally acts as a 
biological driver of CVD mortality and serves primarily as a precision variable in our study.
Bidirected edges are included to account for spatial demographic correlations; for instance, 
racial minorities comprise 22\% of the rural and 43\% of the urban populations 
\citep{castillo2020racial}, and rural counties are known to generally be older whereas 
younger populations are more likely to migrate to urban centers \citep{monnat2025us, cohen2023aging}.

Our model incorporates the missingness mechanism for CVD 
mortality. According to CDC WONDER privacy documentation \citep{cdc_wonder_cmf_doc}, 
mortality data suppression is deterministic: a county's rate is censored 
($R_Y = 1$) if the total recorded deaths $D < 10$. Because total deaths are a
mathematical product of population size ($P$), age structure ($A$), and the age-adjusted mortality rate ($Y$), the suppression rule takes 
the form $R_Y \leftarrow \mathds{1}\{D < 10\}$. Because the missingness of $Y$ is deterministically defined by the unobserved value of $Y$ itself, this is an example of
Missing Not At Random (MNAR) mechanism, illustrated by the red paths in Figure~\ref{fig:dag}. 
We assume population size ($P$) only affects the model through defining urbanicity 
and triggering suppression. As a result, traditional complete-case 
analysis (dropping censored counties) could induce selection bias by deleting the healthiest small counties, which could distort our conclusions.




\section{Data}
\label{data}

We compile 2016 county-level data from multiple public sources, 
merging by FIPS code to create a unified dataset. Our primary 
outcome, age-adjusted CVD mortality per 100,000 people ($Y$), is sourced from 
CDC WONDER \citep{cdc_wonder_cmf_data}. Age adjustment standardizes the outcome 
to the year-2000 national reference population, removing the inherent effect 
of a county's raw age structure \citep{anderson1998age}. For our treatment and 
demographic covariates, we utilize the American Community Survey (ACS) 5-year 
estimates (2012-2016) for the poverty rate ($X$), non-white population percentage 
($C$), and percentage aged 65 and older ($A$). The 5-year ACS estimates are chosen over 1-year estimates because the latter do not provide data 
for geographies with populations under 65,000, which would artificially truncate 
smaller counties. We retain $A$ as a covariate in our models because 
standardizing to a national reference population may not eliminate 
all residual age confounding in counties with extremely skewed demographic profiles.

Uninsurance rates ($M$) are drawn from the Small Area Health Insurance Estimates 
(SAHIE), and state-level Medicaid expansion status ($G$) as of January 1, 2016, is 
obtained from the Kaiser Family Foundation (KFF). Finally, we adopt the 2013 
National Center for Health Statistics (NCHS) urban-rural classification ($R$) 
\citep{ingram20142013}. This six-level ordinal scheme ranges from large central 
metropolitan areas (level 1) to non-core, completely rural counties (level 6). 
This scheme was chosen over raw population density because it better 
captures functional economic integration, commuting patterns, and 
healthcare access zones \citep{office20212020}.

After reconciling census area variations (for example, matching unincorporated Alaskan 
boroughs and independent Virginian cities), our sample 
comprises 3,142 U.S. counties and county-equivalents. Six counties were 
excluded due to missing data not due to suppression. An 
additional 168 counties had their precise CVD mortality rates suppressed due to 
experiencing fewer than 10 recorded cardiovascular deaths in the 2016.

As established in Section~\ref{causal_model}, this deterministic 
suppression results in an MNAR problem. Because the outcome $Y$ 
determines its own missingness $R_Y$, the joint distribution $\mathbb{P}(\mathbf{V})$ 
is not non-parametrically identifiable from the observed data alone. However, the CDC suppression rule ($D < 10$) allows us 
to reframe this missingness as an interval-censoring problem. 
For every suppressed county, the unobserved mortality rate is bounded 
between 0 and a known specific Limit of Detection (LOD). We calculate 
this as $\text{LOD}_i = 900,000 / P_i$, where $P_i$ is the specific county population, bounding the rate just below the 10-death threshold.

We recover the complete joint distribution under parametric modeling assumptions 
using Multiple Imputation by Chained Equations (MICE) paired with a 
left-censored outcome model introduced by \citep{keil2019quantile}. Prior to imputation, counties 
with exactly zero observed deaths (which breaks logarithmic likelihood) were corrected to a nominal rate of $100,000 / P_i$ (representing exactly 1 
theoretical death) for stability purposes. For the 168 suppressed counties, the 
missing outcome values were multiply imputed ($M=5$ datasets, $I=10$ algorithmic 
iterations) using Tobit regression. Each drawn value was 
sampled from a conditional normal distribution that was right-truncated 
at the county's specific $\text{LOD}_i$. The imputation predictor matrix 
included all causal covariates and geographic identifiers, ensuring the randomly
drawn distributions adhered to each county's unique demographic and 
spatial structure. Our final estimation relies on 
the parametric Tobit imputation model's correctness in capturing the tail-end of 
the mortality distribution.

\section{Sensitivity Analysis}
\label{sensitivity}

To compare estimation performane of our left-censored MICE method relative to complete case analysis (CCA) and regular 
multiple imputation, we design and conduct a simulation study. 
Instead of using completely synthetic data, we construct 
semi-synthetic data by preserving the exact empirical covariates  of 
the counties in our 2016 dataset. This ensures that the simulated 
confounding and missingness mechanisms somewhat mimic real-world data.

We simulate an age-adjusted CVD mortality outcome $Y$, the corresponding raw 
(unadjusted) mortality $Y_{\text{raw}}$, and a deterministic missingness 
indicator $R_Y$. Following CDC WONDER documentation \citep{cdc_wonder_cmf_doc}, 
age-adjusted mortality is defined as a standardized weighted 
sum of age-specific raw mortality rates $Y_k$ for age-group $k$.

For simplicity, we stratify the population into two age groups: individuals younger than 65 years old ($k=1$) 
and individuals aged 65 or older ($k=2$). We define the empirical standard 
population weights as $w_1 = \sum (1-A_i)P_i / \sum P_i$ and 
$w_2 = \sum A_i P_i / \sum P_i$, respectively, where $A_i$ is the county 
proportion of residents 65 and older, and $P_i$ is the total county population

For interpretability of our sensitivity analysis, we assume our causal model has a linear structure. In the rest of this paper, we will remove such parametric assumptoins. We generate the true raw CVD mortality rates per 100,000 for each age group 
using intercepts and variances defined to reflect the
higher risk and variance in older populations. For the $<65$ cohort, mortality 
is drawn as:
$$
Y_{\text{raw},1} \sim \mathcal{N}(\mu_1, 200^2)
$$
where $\mu_1 = 100 + 1500X + 500C + 400M - 20R - 20G$. For the $\geq65$ cohort, 
mortality is drawn as:
$$
Y_{\text{raw},2} \sim \mathcal{N}(\mu_2, 400^2)
$$
where $\mu_2 = 1000 + 1500X + 500C + 400M - 20R - 20G$. To ensure
non-negativity of mortality rates, any negative draws are truncated to zero. In this data generating process, the linear causal effect of
poverty ($X$) is fixed at $\beta = 1500$.

The overall crude mortality rate $Y_{\text{raw}}$ is then constructed for each 
county as the local population-weighted average of the two groups: $Y_{\text{raw}}=(1-A)Y_{\text{raw},1}+AY_{\text{raw},2}$.
The final 
observed age-adjusted outcome $Y$ is computed using the aforementioned weights 
$w_1$ and $w_2$: $Y_{\text{raw}}=w_1Y_{\text{raw},1}+w_2Y_{\text{raw},2}$.
Finally, to enforce the CDC suppression mechanism, we 
calculate the expected number of total deaths $D = P \cdot Y_{\text{raw}} / 100,000$. 
The suppression indicator is deterministically assigned as 
$R_Y = \mathds{1}\{D < 10\}$. Any county meeting this criterion has its 
outcome $Y$ set to missing.

Over 100 replications, we apply three estimation 
strategies to the semi-synthetic datasets: (1) CCA, 
which drops all suppressed counties; (2) Standard MICE ($M=5$ imputations); and (3) Left-censored MICE 
($M=5$ imputations) using a Tobit regression model bounded by the deterministic 
Limit of Detection ($\text{LOD}_i = 900,000 / P_i$). For each method, we extract 
the coefficient for poverty ($X$) by fitting ordinary least squares and we calculate the 
estimation bias as $\hat{\beta} - 1500$.

The distribution of estimation bias across the 100 replications is presented 
in Figure~\ref{fig:sensitivity}. The results illustrate the potentially extreme bias that could result from using standard approaches. CCA suffers from selection bias 
because it is forced to drop low-population rural counties, which may alter the covariate distribution. 
Standard MICE also exhibits substantial bias. Because it does not use our knowledge about the suppression mechanism, it uses observed high-mortality counties to impute 
values for suppressed counties that may exceed the ceiling set by the CDC.

In contrast, the left-censored Tobit MICE approach incorporates 
the $D < 10$ bound, forcing the imputation algorithm to draw from the 
appropriate truncated tail of the distribution. While a noticeable amount of bias still remains, the Tobit specification does result in a decrease
in bias and centers the estimate closer to the true parameter, 
justifying its use in our primary empirical analysis.

\section{Causal Quantity of Interest}
\label{quantities}

In this section, we define the causal quantities used to analyze the 
effect of poverty rates on CVD mortality. Because both our treatment ($X$, 
poverty rate) and outcome ($Y$, mortality rate) are continuous, our analysis 
is different from binary treatment frameworks. Rather than 
comparing two levels ($x_1$ versus $x_0$), we estimate the full dose-response function. This allows us to evaluate the 
marginal effect of some shift  $\delta$ at any arbitrary
poverty level $x_0$, such that $x_1 = x_0 + \delta$.

Let $Y_x$ denote the potential outcome—the age-adjusted CVD mortality rate 
that would be observed for a county if its poverty rate were hypothetically 
set to $x$ via an external intervention, holding all else constant. Our 
primary causal quantity
represents the Total Effect ($TE$) of poverty across the support of $X$:
$$TE(x) = \mathbb{E}[Y_x]$$
Here, $\mathbb{E}[Y_x]$ is the expected CVD mortality if a county 
is set to poverty rate $x$ over the distribution of the covariates.

To quantify the magnitude of confounding, we contrast this causal curve with 
the observational Total Variation ($TV$) curve, which is just the conditional 
expectation of the outcome given the observed treatment:
$$TV(x) = \mathbb{E}[Y \mid X = x]$$
The pointwise difference between the observational curve and the causal curve is the Spurious Effect ($SE$), which captures the
association driven by confounding variables. We define the Spurious Effect as:
$$SE(x) = TV(x) - TE(x) = \mathbb{E}[Y \mid X = x] - \mathbb{E}[Y_x]$$
By estimating both $TV(x)$ and $TE(x)$, we can recover $SE(x)$ by 
calculating the vertical distance between the two curves across $X$.

While decomposing the Total Effect into Natural Direct Effects (NDE) and 
Natural Indirect Effects (NIE) through the uninsurance pathway ($M$) is of 
theoretical interest, we focus on the Total Effect in this paper. 
Because accounting for mediation is non-trivial in this setting and remains largely unaddressed in the literature, we leave the question as room for future work.

To non-parametrically identify the continuous Total Effect $TE(x)$ from 
observational data, we invoke the following three  assumptions 
extended for continuous treatments as in \citep{kennedy2017non}. Let $\mathcal{X}$ 
and $\mathcal{Z}$ be the respective support spaces of the treatment and 
covariates.
 
\begin{assumption}[Consistency]
$X=x \rightarrow Y = Y_x$
\end{assumption}

\begin{assumption}[Positivity]
$\pi(x|\mathbf z) \geq \pi_{min} > 0,\forall \ \mathbf z \in \mathcal Z$
\end{assumption}

\begin{assumption}[Ignorability]
$\mathbb E[Y_x|\mathbf Z,X] = \mathbb E[Y_x|\mathbf Z]$
\end{assumption}

Based on the Directed Acyclic Graph (DAG) presented in 
Section~\ref{causal_model}, we construct our adjustment set $\mathbf{Z}$ to 
satisfy the backdoor criterion. The minimal sufficient adjustment set 
to block all backdoor paths is $\{C, R\}$, which severs the confounding 
influence of racial composition ($X \leftarrow C \rightarrow Y$ and 
$X \leftarrow C \rightarrow M \rightarrow Y$) and rurality 
($X \leftarrow R \rightarrow Y$ and $X \leftarrow R \rightarrow M \rightarrow Y$).

However, to improve the statistical efficiency of our continuous estimator, 
we utilize an expanded adjustment set: $\mathbf{Z} = \{C, R, A, G\}$. By 
including the proportion of the population over 65 ($A$) and state Medicaid 
expansion status ($G$) as precision variables, we aim to reduce residual variance in the outcome $Y$, which can tighten our confidence bands without 
adding bias. We exclude the uninsurance rate ($M$) 
from $\mathbf{Z}$. Because $M$ lies on the causal pathway between $X$ and 
$Y$, conditioning on it would block the 
indirect channels that comprise the Total Effect.

Under these assumptions, the continuous causal effect is identified using 
backdoor adjustment, integrating over the marginal distribution of the 
covariates $P(\mathbf{z})$:
$$\mathbb{E}[Y_x] = \int_{\mathcal{Z}} \mathbb{E}[Y \mid X = x, \mathbf{Z} = \mathbf{z}] dP(\mathbf{z})$$

\section{Estimation of Causal Effect}
\label{estimation}


An estimate of the total variation $\widehat{TV}(x)$ can be obtained
with naive non-parametric regression on $X$ alone, unadjusted for any 
other covariates. In our application, we use local linear regression. The optimal bandwidth $h_{TV}^*$ is selected by minimizing the leave-one-out cross-validation score:
\begin{equation*}
\label{eq:cv}
    CV(h) = \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n\Big(\frac{Y_i-\hat{Y}_i(h)}{1-h_{ii}}\Big)^2,
\end{equation*}
where $h_{ii}$ is the $i$-th diagonal element of the hat matrix.

Estimating the total effect $\widehat{TE}(x)$ in the continuous treatment 
setting requires adjustment. We utilize the continuous treatment framework developed by 
\citet{kennedy2017non}. Let $\mu(\mathbf{z}, x) = \mathbb{E}[Y \mid \mathbf{Z} = \mathbf{z}, X = x]$ 
denote the conditional expectation of the outcome. We can rewrite the 
causal effect as the marginalized outcome over the covariate space:
$$\mathbb{E}[Y_x] = \int_{\mathcal{Z}} \mu(\mathbf{z}, x) dP(\mathbf{z})$$

Furthermore, let $\bar{\omega}(x) = \partial \mathbb{P}(X \leq x)/\partial x$ 
denote the marginal treatment density, and $\pi(x \mid \mathbf{z}) = \partial \mathbb{P}(X \leq x \mid \mathbf{Z} = \mathbf{z}) / \partial x$ 
denote the conditional treatment density. 
We construct a doubly robust function $\xi(\mathbf{V}; \pi, \mu)$ 
of the observed data $\mathbf{V}$ and nuisance functions $(\pi, \mu)$ such that 
its expectation equals causal parameter $\psi$, given that at least one 
nuisance function is consistently estimated, so that the influence function has mean zero.

\begin{theorem}[Efficient Influence Function \citep{kennedy2017non}]
    Under a non-parametric model, the efficient influence function for 
    $\psi = \int_{\mathcal{X}} \int_{\mathcal{Z}} \mu(\mathbf{z}, x) \bar{\omega}(x) dP(\mathbf{z}) dx$ 
    is defined as $\xi(\mathbf{V}; \pi, \mu) - \psi + \int_{\mathcal{X}} \{ \mu(\mathbf{Z}, x) - \int_{\mathcal{Z}} \mu(\mathbf{z}, x) dP(\mathbf{z}) \} \bar{\omega}(x) dx$, 
    where the pseudo-outcome is:
    \begin{multline*}
        \xi(\mathbf{V}; \pi, \mu) = \frac{Y - \mu(\mathbf{Z}, X)}{\pi(X \mid \mathbf{Z})} \int_{\mathcal{Z}} \pi(X \mid \mathbf{z}) dP(\mathbf{z}) \\
        + \int_{\mathcal{Z}} \mu(\mathbf{z}, X) dP(\mathbf{z})
    \end{multline*}
\end{theorem}

To construct the empirical pseudo-outcome, we estimate the nuisance 
functions $\hat{\mu}$ and $\hat{\pi}$. For the conditional expectation 
$\hat{\mu}(\mathbf{Z}, X)$, we utilize the \texttt{SuperLearner} ensemble 
algorithm \citep{SuperLearner} with 10-fold cross-validation on
XGBoost. As detailed in \citet{cdc_wonder_cmf_doc}, rates derived from 
low death counts are more variable than those from high counts. To account for heteroskedasticity, we weight the objective functions 
of all machine learning models by the total county population $P_i$.

Estimating the conditional density $\hat{\pi}(x \mid \mathbf{z})$ can be difficult in continuous settings. We break this into a two-stage 
location-scale model. First, we regress $X$ on $\mathbf{Z}$ using a SuperLearner 
to obtain the conditional mean $\hat{\mu}_X(\mathbf{Z}) = \mathbb{E}[X \mid \mathbf{Z}]$ 
and extract the residuals $\hat{\epsilon} = X - \hat{\mu}_X(\mathbf{Z})$. 
Second, we regress the squared residuals on $\mathbf{Z}$ to estimate the 
conditional variance $\hat{\sigma}^2_X(\mathbf{Z}) = \max(\mathbb{E}[\hat{\epsilon}^2 \mid \mathbf{Z}], 10^{-4})$, 
where the lower bound is enforced to prevent numerical instability in the density 
ratio.

We estimate the 
conditional density non-parametrically instead of using Gaussian density assumptions. We standardize the treatment residuals 
as $\tilde{\epsilon}_i = \hat{\epsilon}_i / \hat{\sigma}_X(\mathbf{Z}_i)$ 
and apply Kernel Density Estimation (KDE). An estimate of the conditional density interpolated
at each county's observed treatment is obtained with cubic smoothing splines 
evaluated over the KDE space.

Using empirical measures $\mathbb{P}_n$, we generate counterfactual matrices 
evaluating $\hat{\mu}$ and $\hat{\pi}$ for all $n$ counties across a fixed 
grid $\mathcal{X}_{\text{eval}}$. Taking the column means is equivalent to integrating out the 
covariates over the empirical measure,
giving us our marginal quantities for $x \in \mathcal{X}_{\text{eval}}$:
\begin{align*}
    \int_{\mathcal{Z}} \hat{\mu}(\mathbf{z}, x) d\mathbb{P}_n(\mathbf{z}) &= \frac{1}{n} \sum_{j=1}^n \hat{\mu}(\mathbf{Z}_j, x) \\
    \int_{\mathcal{Z}} \hat{\pi}(x \mid \mathbf{z}) d\mathbb{P}_n(\mathbf{z}) &= \frac{1}{n} \sum_{j=1}^n \hat{\pi}(x \mid \mathbf{Z}_j)
\end{align*}
The final empirical 
pseudo-outcome is constructed as:
\begin{multline*}
\label{eq:pseudo}
    \hat{\xi}_i = \frac{Y_i - \hat{\mu}(\mathbf{Z}_i, X_i)}{\hat{\pi}(X_i \mid \mathbf{Z}_i)} \int_{\mathcal{Z}} \hat{\pi}(X_i \mid \mathbf{z}) d\mathbb{P}_n(\mathbf{z}) \\
    + \int_{\mathcal{Z}} \hat{\mu}(\mathbf{z}, X_i) d\mathbb{P}_n(\mathbf{z}), \ i=1,...,n
\end{multline*}
We then regress $\hat{\xi}_i$ on $X_i$ using local linear regression with an 
internally optimized cross-validated bandwidth $h_{TE}^*$ to produce the 
final continuous causal curve $\widehat{TE}(x)$.

To quantify uncertainty (again following \citet{kennedy2017non}), we compute a sandwich variance estimator from the empirical score equations of the local linear regressions. 
For a given evaluation point $x$, let $\mathbf{D}_h$ represent the empirical 
design matrix of the kernel weights, and let $\mathbf{S}_i$ denote the 
estimated influence function components for the $i$-th county, which includes 
the regression score altered by the numerical integral of the nuisance 
corrections. The pointwise variance for the $m$-th imputed dataset is 
calculated as $\hat{\Sigma}^{(m)}(x) = \text{Cov}(\mathbf{D}_h^{-1} \mathbf{S})$.

The entire procedure is executed the same across all $M=5$ left-censored 
imputed datasets, and we then aggregate the pointwise curve estimates using Rubin's 
Rules \citep{rubin1987multiple}. The pooled point estimate is 
$\widehat{TE}(x) = \frac{1}{M}\sum_{m=1}^M \widehat{TE}^{(m)}(x)$. 
The total pointwise variance is composed of the average 
within-imputation variance ($W$) and the between-imputation variance ($B$):
$$V_{TE}(x) = W_{TE}(x) + \left(1 + \frac{1}{M}\right)B_{TE}(x)$$
The final 95\% confidence bands at each evaluation point are constructed as 
$\widehat{TE}(x) \pm 1.96 \sqrt{V_{TE}(x)}$.

We also calculate the 
pointwise variance for the unadjusted total variation ($\widehat{TV}$). This 
procedure is similar to the $\widehat{TE}$ variance estimation, pooling the 
unadjusted local linear regressions across the $M=5$ imputations using the same 
application of Rubin's Rules.
Because the total variation is a naive regression of $Y$ on $X$, its residual 
error takes in the unadjusted variance driven by the covariates 
in $\mathbf{Z}$. As shown in our results, the confidence 
bands for the observational total variation ($\widehat{TV}$) are consequently wider 
than those of the doubly robust total effect ($\widehat{TE}$), especially at extreme 
high values of CVD mortality where we would expect the structural variance of the outcome to be larger.

a steep positive slope, whereas the naive estimate implies a dropoff past a certain poverty rate, 
showing that the other covariates ($\mathbf{Z}$) negatively confound the 
relationship and change our conclusion about how extreme 
poverty rates affect cardiovascular mortality.



 

\section{Causal Effect Heterogeneity}
\label{heterogeneity}
As an alternative approach, we analyze heterogeneity in the causal effect by applying a 
continuous-treatment causal forest using the \texttt{grf} package \citep{grf}. 

When the treatment is continuous, the \texttt{grf} algorithm estimates the 
conditional Average Partial Effect ($APE$), defined as the localized ratio of 
covariance to variance:
$$\tau(\mathbf{z}) = \frac{\text{Cov}[Y, X \mid \mathbf{Z} = \mathbf{z}]}{\text{Var}[X \mid \mathbf{Z} = \mathbf{z}]}$$
Under the assumption of no confounding, this represents the individualized 
treatment effect given a specific covariate profile $\mathbf{z}$. Taking the mean of these individualized treatment effects over our 
observed data ($\frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n \hat \tau(Z_i)$) yields the following global interpretation: on average, across 
all counties in our dataset, a 1\% increase in poverty is associated with a 
5.06 increase in age-adjusted CVD deaths per 100,000 people.

To account for the uncertainty introduced by missing data, we fit 
separate causal forests across the $m=5$ multiply imputed datasets. For each 
imputation, we computed the unit-level conditional average treatment effects. Similarly to our methodology in Section~\ref{estimation}, these estimates were then aggregated across the imputations for each 
county to obtain a stabilized, pooled estimate of $\tau(\mathbf{z})$.

Again, isolating causal quantities beyond $TE$, including the specific effect of treatment 
on the treated ($ETT$), is either non-trivial or has a different interpretation in a continuous treatment setting.
Although we acknowledge that binarization results in some loss of continuous variance, 
we discretize the poverty rate here to obtain a quantity more aligned with the traditional $ETT(x) = \mathbb E[Y_{x_1}-Y_{x_0}|X=x]$ estimate, which would be difficult otherwise. We split the poverty rate into two groups, designating the set of all rates above 
the national median as $\mathcal X_{\text{high}}$ for ``High Poverty" (treated) and the set of all rates below the median 
as $\mathcal X_{\text{low} }$ for ``Low Poverty" (untreated). We then compute the average marginal effect 
within each group. Thus, we are estimating $$\mathbb E[\tau(\mathbf Z)|X \in \mathcal X_{\text{high}}] \quad \text{and} \quad
\mathbb E[\tau(\mathbf Z)|X \in \mathcal X_{\text{low}}].$$
These results are detailed in Table~\ref{tab:ett}.

We observe that the average marginal effect is slightly higher for counties 
with poverty rates below the median. While in 
Section~\ref{estimation}, we demonstrated a linear global dose-response, 
this localized forest analysis suggests a subtle variation in the slope: 
marginal increases in poverty have a steeper impact in 
lower-poverty counties, but the incremental effect is reduced 
in regions where the poverty is already high. Note that the average marginal effect is analogous to the local 
slope of the dose-response curve rather than the absolute outcome level.
In other words, this suggests a mild diminishing marginal impact: once poverty is 
already widespread and baseline mortality is high, further incremental shifts 
in poverty may yield a smaller (though still positive) impact on CVD 
mortality.

\input{manuscript/tables/ett}

We further investigate the $z-TE$s, which is the average partial effect 
evaluated at specific levels of elements of $\mathbf{Z}$: $$\mathbb{E}[\tau(\mathbf{Z}) \mid Z_j = z], \quad \text{with} \quad\mathbf{Z} = \{Z_j\}_{j=1}^p$$ for $p$ covariates. We conduct this 
analysis for all four of our primary confounders and precision variables. For 
continuous covariates, we partition observations into deciles and compute the 
mean estimated treatment effect within each bin, applying corresponding 
uncertainty bands based on standard errors. Figure~\ref{fig:z_te} presents 
the results of these investigations.


Starting with the proportion of non-White 
residents, we observe an initial increase in the treatment effect across the 
lower and middle deciles, followed by a sharp decline in the highest deciles (above 
approximately the 7th decile). The initial upward trend suggests that as the minority population grows from low to 
moderate levels, the intersection of racial inequities and economic 
disparities work synergistically, increasing the harmful effects of poverty. However, in the highest deciles—representing extremely segregated or 
majority-minority counties—the marginal effect of poverty drops suddenly. 
This implies that racism may elevate 
the baseline CVD mortality risk so much that additional 
differences in poverty level are diminished in importance.

The decile of the proportion of the county aged 65 
and above exhibits an initially increasing relationship with the $z-TE$, 
followed by a steep decrease at the upper deciles. 
Younger populations are inherently less susceptible to CVD, which could limit 
the socio-environmental impacts of poverty. As the population 
begins to age, poverty-related stressors—such as housing instability, poor 
nutrition, and lack of coverage—exacerbate developing CVD risks. However, the pronounced 
drop-off at the extreme highest deciles seems to align with a biological 
ceiling, where in the most elderly counties, advanced age becomes the 
overwhelming, dominant driver of cardiovascular mortality. Once a population 
crosses this biological threshold, the marginal predictive power of socioeconomic 
status becomes much less impactful.

The urban-rural classification scheme demonstrates a 
mainly positive association with the $z-TE$, though with notable 
non-linear variations. We observe a slight spike at category 2,
which are defined as large fringe metropolitan/suburban counties, followed by a localized decrease 
between categories 5 and 6, defined as micropolitan to non-core transitions \citep{ingram20142013}. The overall positive trend validates our 
hypothesis that spatial inequalities, such as provider shortages and 
transportation barriers, amplify the effect of poverty in rural 
settings. The localized spike at category 2 may reflect a unique phenomenon of suburban poverty, where low-income populations are isolated from the public safety nets and transit systems which are found in denser urban cores (designated as category 1). Although the trend is higher rurality for higher scheme levels, the urban-rural classification scheme may not be best considered as a strictly ordinal factor. Attributes of each level that are most linked with poverty and CVD mortality may not change monotonically with this scheme This could explain the small variations that we observe in the curve's direction.
Despite the slight fluctuation in the middle categories and drop off for higher categories, the general trend
remains clear: poverty increases in its strength as a driver of CVD mortality as counties become 
more isolated.

Lastly, and somewhat counterintuitively, we observe 
that counties located in states that have expanded Medicaid exhibit a higher 
$z-TE$ than those in non-expansion states. While one might assume expansion would flatten the effect of poverty, this 
result could indicate a shift in baseline mortality risk. Note that many people may be eligible for Medicaid without falling below the poverty-line. In non-expansion 
states, the broad restriction of healthcare access might actually lead to elevated CVD mortality 
across more income levels in each county. This could potentially flatten the local slope if low to moderate-income populations are affected by lack of Medicaid, or even other harmful policies that are correlated with lack of Medicaid. Conversely, if 
Medicaid expansion successfully lowers the baseline mortality floor by protecting 
low-income adults, children, pregnant women, elderly patients, and those with disability, the remaining CVD mortality burden may fall to those who are far below the poverty line and who face barriers beyond inability to pay for insurance, such as transportation, nutrition, and perhaps even social boundaries. As a consequence, while general CVD 
outcomes may improve in expansion states, extreme poverty remains a strong predictive 
factor for cardiovascular death.

These heterogeneity results show that the effect of poverty is not uniform across counties, but instead varies with demographic composition, age structure, geographic context, and policy environment.

\section{Discussion}
\label{discussion}

Throughout this investigation, we have examined the causal relationship 
between poverty and cardiovascular disease (CVD) mortality from multiple 
methodological angles, and we have isolated the total effect from demographic and geographic confounders. Using doubly 
robust estimation, we showed a linear dose-response relationship: as 
county-level poverty increases, age-adjusted CVD mortality increases at a constant rate. Furthermore, by contrasting this causal curve with a 
naive observational estimate, we demonstrated that raw demographic variation masks that the CVD penalty of poverty is harsher than we originally thought. Our localized causal forest suggested that the marginal effect of poverty has interesting relationships with our covariates of interest.

A key 
limitation of this study is our reliance on a parametric model to handle 
interval-censored mortality outcomes. While the Centers for Disease Control and 
Prevention's suppression criterion (censoring death counts below 10) is a 
known quantity, the recovery of the full outcome distribution depends 
on our multiple imputation modeling assumptions. If the true 
distribution of these counts deviates significantly from those imputed, our estimates may carry unrecognized bias at the lower tail. Although on average, this method resulted in less bias than others in our sensitivity analysis, it was not completely infallible, which motivates research into better solutions to address outcome data suppression.

As with all causal inference, our estimates 
rely on the assumption of unconfoundedness after having accounted for $\mathbf Z$. While we 
adjusted for a large and informed set of covariates—including racial composition, 
age structure, rurality, and Medicaid expansion—there may be unmeasured confounding. Currently unobserved variables such as differences in nutrition from location (food deserts, for example \citep{karpyn2019changing, kelli2019living}), environmental pollution exposure, and county-level behavioral health trends (smoking and physical activity rates) may intersect with both poverty and CVD risk, which would bias our estimates of the causal effect.

There are several interesting directions for future research. One is the formal decomposition of the 
total causal effect into the natural direct effect and the natural indirect effect, 
which operates through the mediating pathway of uninsurance rates in our causal model.  Estimating this 
mediation could be approached in two ways: either by binarizing the poverty rate 
and applying the mediation analysis methods developed by 
\citet{farbmacher2022causal}, or by developing non-parametric, doubly robust continuous mediation estimators 
akin to the total effect framework established by \citet{kennedy2017non}, although this is not trivial.

In conclusion, these findings have implications for public health policy. 
By demonstrating that poverty linearly exacerbates CVD mortality, 
and that this effect interacts with confounders in surprising ways, our results are evidence against uniform covariate-blind health interventions. This could inform
policymakers to
recognize that poverty is a major cardiovascular risk factor.
